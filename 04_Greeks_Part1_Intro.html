What Are the Greeks?

The Greeks measure the sensitivity of an option's price (or an entire book) to specific market parameters. They are the risk manager's control panel — each Greek is a partial derivative of the pricing function with respect to one input variable.

🎯 Interview framing: "Greeks decompose market risk into orthogonal buckets. Delta tells me my equity exposure, Vega my vol exposure, Theta my time cost. Running a book means making sure each Greek is where you want it to be — either hedged out or expressing a deliberate view."

Qualitative vs Quantitative Understanding
Qualitative — Market Intuition

Understanding that as an option approaches expiry, time value disappears (Theta). That being long a call means you benefit from a rally (positive Delta). That vol spikes make your long options worth more (positive Vega). This is what gets you through 80% of interview questions.

Quantitative — Precise Model Output

The exact partial derivatives from Black-Scholes (or local vol / Heston) that let you hedge precisely. Managing a complex book requires N(d₁) to 4 decimal places, not just "roughly 0.5." Intuition sets the direction; the model sets the hedge ratio.

Taylor Series — Why It Matters for Option Pricing

The Taylor series is the mathematical bridge between abstract partial derivatives (the Greeks) and practical P&L approximation. It allows traders to estimate how their portfolio will change without re-running the full pricing model for every market move.

\[ V(S + \Delta S, t + \Delta t, \sigma + \Delta\sigma) \approx V + \underbrace{\Delta \cdot \Delta S}_{\text{1st order}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\Gamma(\Delta S)^2}_{\text{2nd order}} + \underbrace{\Theta \cdot \Delta t}_{\text{time}} + \underbrace{\nu \cdot \Delta\sigma}_{\text{vol}} + \cdots \]

First-Order vs Second-Order Approximation
1st-Order (Linear)
\[\Delta V \approx \Delta \cdot \Delta S\]

Represents the option price change with a straight line. Accurate only for small spot moves. As ΔS grows, the error becomes significant because it ignores the curvature of the option payoff.

2nd-Order (Parabolic)
\[\Delta V \approx \Delta \cdot \Delta S + \frac{1}{2}\Gamma(\Delta S)^2\]

Adds the convexity correction. The ½Γ(ΔS)² term captures the curvature of the option price. This is why long-gamma traders love big moves — the Gamma term is always positive for long options.

The Gamma P&L Formula — Core Volatility Arbitrage Insight

When you delta-hedge an option position continuously, your directional exposure is neutralized. The residual P&L over an infinitesimal time step \(dt\) is purely driven by the variance spread:

\[ d\Pi = \frac{1}{2}\,\Gamma\,S^2\,(\sigma_R^2 - \sigma_{\text{IV}}^2)\,dt \]
Deep Dive: Decomposing the Formula & Core Concepts

This formula is the core of derivatives market-making. Let's break down why this is a pure volatility arbitrage:

  • No Directional Term (\(\Delta S\)): By continuously adjusting your shares of the underlying to match Delta (\(\Delta = \partial V/\partial S\)), the linear spot price exposure is entirely removed. There is no \(dS\) term in the P&L.
  • Gamma (\(\Gamma\)) acts as the Leverage: Gamma determines how quickly your Delta changes. It is the scale of your exposure to spot price acceleration. Higher Gamma means larger hedging P&L.
  • Volatility (Variance) Spread (\(\sigma_R^2 - \sigma_{\text{IV}}^2\)): The core bet. You are trading **Realized Volatility (\(\sigma_R\))** against the **Implied Volatility (\(\sigma_{\text{IV}}\))** at which you bought or sold the option.
Conceptual Breakdown of Key Terms:
1. "Delta-Hedge Continuously" & Neutralized Directional Exposure

Delta-hedging involves offsetting the option's directional risk by holding a share position of size \(-\Delta\). For instance, if you are long 1 Call option with \(\Delta = 0.60\), you short \(0.60\) shares of the underlying stock. As the stock price moves, the gain/loss on the option is offset by the loss/gain on the stock. By continuously rebalancing this share quantity as \(\Delta\) changes, you eliminate all exposure to directional price moves (the first-order derivative term is zero).

2. "Residual P&L" (The Gamma vs. Theta Tug of War)

Although direction-neutral, a delta-hedged portfolio's value is not static. It experiences two opposing forces over time:

  • Gamma (\(\Gamma > 0\) for long options): Benefits you. As the stock moves, your delta changes in a way that makes you buy low and sell high (known as Gamma Scalping), locking in profits.
  • Theta (\(\Theta < 0\) for long options): Costs you. Option value decays as time expires.

The residual P&L is the net outcome of these two opposing effects: daily Gamma scalping gains minus daily Theta time decay.

3. "Infinitesimal Time Step" (\(dt\)) & Black-Scholes Relationship

In continuous time, the change in the portfolio over a tiny period \(dt\) is: \[ d\Pi = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma\,(dS)^2 + \Theta\,dt \] Under the Black-Scholes framework, the option's theta is theoretically linked to its gamma by \(\Theta = -\frac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2 \sigma_{\text{IV}}^2\). Also, the actual realized stock price variance over \(dt\) is \((dS)^2 = S^2 \sigma_R^2 dt\). Substituting these into the equation reveals that the net P&L is driven entirely by the difference between realized volatility and implied volatility.

4. "Variance Spread" (\(\sigma_R^2 - \sigma_{\text{IV}}^2\))

This spread represents the core volatility bet. Implied variance (\(\sigma_{\text{IV}}^2\)) is the market's priced expectation of future volatility (the "cost" of the hedge through Theta). Realized variance (\(\sigma_R^2\)) is the actual volatility delivered by the stock (the "yield" of the hedge through Gamma scalping). You are trading the difference between these two variances.

Position TypeGamma SignP&L ConditionMarket View
Long Option (Buyer) \(\Gamma > 0\) (Positive) Makes money if **\(\sigma_R > \sigma_{\text{IV}}\)** The stock moves *more* than the option price implied. Hedging gains exceed Theta decay.
Short Option (Seller) \(\Gamma < 0\) (Negative) Makes money if **\(\sigma_R < \sigma_{\text{IV}}\)** The stock moves *less* than the option price implied. Theta decay collected exceeds hedging losses.

Interview Takeaway: "Delta-hedging converts a directional bet into a pure variance arbitrage. If I am long Gamma, I make money if realized variance beats the implied variance I purchased, regardless of direction. If I am short Gamma, I am harvesting the premium and betting realized stays below implied."

Static vs Dynamic Hedging

All hedging strategies sit on a spectrum between fully static (set-and-forget) and fully dynamic (continuous rebalancing). Understanding this trade-off is fundamental to derivatives trading.

⚓ Static Hedge

  • No adjustment after initiation
  • Model-independent — price = cost of hedge
  • Perfect replication where applicable
  • Low operational overhead
  • Works for linear or deterministic payoffs
📌 Example: Hedging a digital option at expiry with a call spread. Once set up, no rebalancing needed. The hedge automatically delivers the payoff at maturity.

⚡ Dynamic Hedge

  • Continuous rebalancing required as market evolves
  • Model-dependent — hedge ratio from BS or local vol
  • Approximate replication between re-hedges
  • Transaction costs, slippage, execution risk
  • Necessary for nonlinear payoffs (options)
⚡ The cost: In theory, continuous hedging costs exactly the option premium. In practice: bid/ask spread on every trade, market impact, gap risk overnight. These frictions make dynamic hedging an art, not just a formula.
The Real World — A Hybrid Approach

In practice, structured products use both:

Static Component
Fixed at inception
Replicates deterministic payoffs. Zero rebalancing cost. Example: a strip of vanilla options hedging a barrier's terminal payoff.
Dynamic Component
Adjusted throughout life
Manages specific sensitivities (Δ, ν) that can't be locked in statically. Driven by the Greeks of the residual risk.
🎯 Goal of dynamic hedging: Transform unpredictable market movements into predictable, hedgeable risks. After delta-hedging, your P&L is no longer driven by "which way the market moves" but by whether realised vol beats your implied vol. You've converted directional risk into a pure vol bet.
