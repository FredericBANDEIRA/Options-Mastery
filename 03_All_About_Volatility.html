Why It Matters
Stock Price Evolution — The SDE

The log-price of a stock follows a Brownian motion with drift. This is the continuous-time foundation of all derivatives pricing:

\[ d\log(S_t) = \underbrace{\left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)dt}_{\text{Drift (signal)}} + \underbrace{\sigma\, dW_t}_{\text{Vol (noise)}} \]
Drift Term — The Signal

Deterministic component. The stock's long-run expected growth rate, net of the volatility drag penalty (−σ²/2). This is where the asset "wants" to go on average.

\[\mu_g = \mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\]
Volatility Term — The Noise

Stochastic component driven by Brownian motion \(dW_t\). Represents random market shocks. Every infinitesimal price tick contains this noise scaled by \(\sigma\). It cannot be predicted — only measured and hedged.

🎯 Interview line: "The drift is the signal — the long-term trend. The volatility term is the noise — the uncertainty that prevents the asset from following a perfectly straight line. In options, we don't bet on the drift; we bet on the noise."

Realized vs Implied — The Core Distinction
Realized Volatility (RV / HV)

Backward-looking. Computed from historical price data. Objective, model-free. Tells you how much the asset actually moved. Used to assess whether you paid too much or too little for an option.

✓ Objective    ✓ Historical    ✗ Predictive
Implied Volatility (IV)

Forward-looking. Backed out of market option prices via Black-Scholes inversion. Subjective, model-dependent. Tells you what the market expects volatility to be. The price of uncertainty.

✓ Forward-looking    ✓ Market consensus    ✗ Model-dependent
The Volatility Risk Premium (VRP)

Historically, implied volatility has almost always been higher than subsequent realized volatility. This gap is the Volatility Risk Premium — the "insurance premium" investors pay for protection.

\[\text{VRP} = \mathbb{E}[\text{IV}] - \mathbb{E}[\text{RV}] > 0\]

💰 The vol selling trade: Because IV > RV on average, systematically selling options (collecting IV) and delta-hedging (paying RV in hedge costs) generates a positive carry. This is the basis of volatility selling strategies — but they suffer catastrophic losses during vol spikes (2008, Mar 2020, Aug 2024).
Implied Volatility — The Market's Forward-Looking Plug

Implied Volatility is the value of σ that, when plugged into the Black-Scholes formula, makes the theoretical price equal to the observed market price. It is solved by numerical inversion — there is no closed-form solution.

\[ C_{\text{market}} = \text{BS}(S, K, r, T, \sigma_{\text{implied}}) \implies \sigma_{\text{IV}} = \text{BS}^{-1}(C_{\text{market}}) \]
The Rebonato Quote — Why BS if It's Wrong?
"Implied volatility is the wrong number you put in the wrong formula to get the right price."

— Riccardo Rebonato

🧠 Interview answer — "Why use BS if it's wrong?"
"We use it as a mapping tool — a common language to translate option prices into a standardized volatility number. Like quoting bond yields instead of prices. Everyone knows BS is wrong, but it gives us a consistent coordinate system to locate where an option sits on the volatility surface, compare relative value across strikes and expiries, and communicate risk in a model-independent way."

Volatility Skew — Crash-o-Phobia Quantified

Black-Scholes assumes a flat vol for all strikes. Reality disagrees. The skew is the slope of the IV curve as a function of strike — and it encodes the market's fear of directional moves.

FX Smile — Symmetric Fear

IV is higher for both low AND high strikes. The market fears large moves in either direction. EUR/USD could gap up or gap down. Curve looks like a "U" — a genuine smile.

Equity Skew — Directional Fear (Crash-o-Phobia)

IV is much higher for low strikes (OTM puts). The market prices in the fear of crashes much more than rallies. Institutional demand for downside protection creates a persistent left-skew. It's asymmetric by design.

Measuring the Skew — Two Approaches
1. Delta Skew — The Theoretical Gold Standard
\[\text{Delta Skew} = \sigma_{\text{25-delta put}} - \sigma_{\text{25-delta call}}\]

Compares the IV of a 25-delta* put (OTM protection, roughly \(K \approx S \cdot e^{-0.5\sigma\sqrt{T}}\)) against a 25-delta* call (OTM upside). In equity markets, this is always positive because left-tail protection is more expensive.

* 25-Delta: Options with a delta of \(\pm 0.25\) (roughly a 25% probability of expiring in-the-money). Using delta instead of absolute strikes standardizes moneyness across different assets, naturally scaling with volatility and time to maturity.

Normalised version (best for cross-asset comparison):

\[\text{Normalised Skew} = \frac{\sigma_{25P} - \sigma_{25C}}{\sigma_{\text{ATM}}}\]
💡 Dividing by ATM vol strips out the "level effect" — you're measuring pure skew, independent of how expensive vol is overall. This allows you to compare skew on Apple vs Gold vs the S&P.
2. Strike Skew — The Practitioner's Favourite
\[\text{Strike Skew} = \sigma(90\%) - \sigma_{\text{ATM}}\]

* Notation: \(\sigma(90\%)\) represents the implied volatility of an option with a strike price at 90% of the current stock price (e.g., a strike of $90 when stock is at $100). \(\sigma_{\text{ATM}}\) is At-The-Money volatility (where strike = 100%).

Or alternatively: \(\sigma(90\%) - \sigma(110\%)\) — comparing a 10% crash strike vs a 10% rally strike. This is highly intuitive because you can read it directly from a volatility surface screenshot without calculating options deltas.

⚠️
Never normalize strike skew by ATM vol. Here is a clear numerical example of the pitfall:
  • Normal Regime: ATM Vol = \(20\%\), and 90% Strike Vol = \(25\%\).
    • Absolute Skew = \(25\% - 20\% = 5\%\) (5 vols).
    • Normalized Skew = \(5\% / 20\% = 25\%\).
  • Crisis Regime (e.g., March 2020): ATM Vol explodes to \(60\%\), and 90% Strike Vol spikes to \(70\%\).
    • Absolute Skew = \(70\% - 60\% = 10\%\) (10 vols). In real dollar terms, the cost of downside protection has doubled.
    • Normalized Skew = \(10\% / 60\% \approx 16.7\%\).
  • The Illusion: If you only monitor the normalized skew, it appears to have decreased from \(25\%\) to \(16.7\%\), falsely signaling that downside panic has cooled. This mathematical compression is a dangerous trap for risk managers.

In currency markets, there is no single directional "default" or "crash" bias as there is in equities. A currency pair can gap violently in either direction (e.g., USD spikes vs EUR, or EUR spikes vs USD). Therefore, both OTM calls and OTM puts carry a volatility premium to protect against sudden two-sided tail events.

Smile-Adjusted Delta

In practice, when spot moves, the vol surface moves with it. The standard BS Delta misses this "shadow" effect:

\[ \Delta_{\text{smile}} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial C}{\partial S}}_{\text{BS Delta}} + \underbrace{\frac{\partial C}{\partial \sigma} \cdot \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial S}}_{\text{Shadow Delta (Vega × Skew slope)}} \]
The Vega Contribution

The second term is Vega × skew slope. In equity markets, when spot falls, IV rises (negative ∂σ/∂S). This means the true delta is smaller than BS suggests — you need fewer shares in your hedge than BS says.

Practical Implication

Delta-hedgers running equity put books typically use a lower delta than Black-Scholes because they know that if spot falls, vol will spike — partially offsetting the put's intrinsic gain. Using raw BS delta over-hedges.

\[\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial S} \approx \frac{\delta\sigma}{\delta K}\bigg|_{\text{skew slope}}\]
Term Structure of Skew — Why Front-Month Skew Is Steeper

Short-dated skew is systematically steeper than long-dated skew. Four reasons:

MechanismExplanation
Jump Risk Concentration A 10% weekly move is a "crash." Over 2 years, the market has time to recover — the fear of any specific strike is "smeared" across the curve.
Gamma Effect Short-dated options have very high Gamma. If a market maker is short front-month puts and the market drops, hedging requirements explode. To compensate, they charge much more for those strikes.
Front-Loaded VRP Investors pay most for immediate protection. The "fire extinguisher premium" is highest when you can smell smoke now, not in 2 years.
Vol-of-Vol Short-term IV is more volatile than long-term IV. This "uncertainty about uncertainty" makes the near-term skew curve sharper and more kinked.
Index Skew vs Single Stock Skew
Equity Index (S&P 500, Eurostoxx)
  • Higher skew — persistent institutional demand for crash protection
  • Lower curvature — diversification means no single-stock jump risk
  • Slow to "crash to zero" — large cap diversification buffers
  • Institutional put-buying creates structural skew supply
Single Stock (AAPL, TSLA…)
  • Lower skew — less directional crash demand
  • Higher curvature — "jump-to-default" and earnings-gap risk sharpens the wings
  • Fraud, M&A, earnings miss can send a stock to near-zero
  • Both wings (puts AND calls) expensive — M&A rumour can cause a 40% up-move
🔑 High-probability interview question: "Why does the S&P have higher skew but lower curvature than a single stock?" — Diversification removes idiosyncratic jump risk (less curvature) but leaves systemic crash risk fully intact, with strong structural demand from institutional hedgers (higher skew).
Smile Curvature — The "U" Shape of Tail Risk

If skew is the slope of the vol surface, curvature is its bend. It's the second derivative of IV with respect to strike — and it captures the market's demand for protection against extreme "black swan" events in both directions.

\[\text{Curvature} \sim \frac{\partial^2\,\sigma_{\text{imp}}(K)}{\partial K^2}\]
Low Strikes (OTM Puts)
IV rises sharply
Sellers of crash protection demand massive premium for "below zero" type risks
High Strikes (OTM Calls)
IV flattens then rises
Short-squeeze / M&A gap-up risk — the right wing re-inflates
Measuring Curvature — The Discrete Second Derivative
\[\text{Skew Convexity} \approx \frac{\sigma(90\%) + \sigma(110\%) - 2\,\sigma(100\%)}{(\Delta\text{Strike})^2}\]

Interpretation: how much the "wings" (90% and 110% strikes) overshoot the ATM vol relative to what a linear skew would predict. Higher = more fat-tail premium = more "U" shape.

💡 This is mathematically analogous to bond convexity: just as bond convexity measures the second-order sensitivity of price to yield, skew convexity measures the second-order sensitivity of IV to strike. Both convexities are desirable and therefore have a cost.

The 4 Pillars of a Valid IV Surface

For a volatility surface to be arbitrage-free, it must satisfy four constraints. Violating any one creates a risk-free profit opportunity:

Pillar 1 — Call Spreads Must Be Positive (C₁ ≥ C₂ for K₁ < K₂)
\[C(K_1) \geq C(K_2) \quad \text{for } K_1 < K_2\]

Logic: As K increases, the right to buy is worth less. A call at K=100 must be worth more than a call at K=110. If not, buy the 110-call and sell the 100-call → immediate risk-free profit.

Implication for the surface: IV can be higher for lower strikes, but the option dollar price must still decrease as K increases. The dollar price slope must be negative in K.

Pillar 2 — Call Spread Ratio Bounded by 1 (No "Super-Replication")
\[0 \leq \frac{C(K_1) - C(K_2)}{K_2 - K_1} \leq 1\]

Logic: A call spread's maximum payoff is the strike difference (K₂ − K₁). If the spread's price exceeds this, you can pocket instant arbitrage: sell the spread and invest the difference.

Interpretation: Formally, this means the slope of the call price with respect to K is between −1 and 0. It bounds the implied risk-neutral probability of the underlying exceeding the strikes.

Pillar 3 — Calendar Spreads Must Be Positive (C(T₂) ≥ C(T₁) for T₂ > T₁)
\[C(K, T_2) \geq C(K, T_1) \quad \text{for } T_2 > T_1\]

Logic: More time = more optionality. A 12-month call should always be worth at least as much as a 6-month call at the same strike (for a non-dividend stock). If not, buy the long-dated call, sell the short-dated call → free money when the short-dated expires.

Exception: Large discrete dividends can violate this for deep ITM calls (but almost never in practice for ATM options).

Pillar 4 — Butterfly Spreads Must Be Positive (Convexity in K)
\[C(K-\epsilon) + C(K+\epsilon) - 2C(K) \geq 0\]

Logic: The option price must be convex in the strike price. This is the hardest constraint. If a butterfly spread has negative price, there exists a "negative probability" mass in the implied risk-neutral distribution — a mathematical impossibility.

🚩 Most commonly violated in poorly calibrated models. When a vol model is calibrated too aggressively to fit market prices, it can create a non-convex price curve — a "dip" that implies negative probabilities. Any model used for production surface calibration (SVI, SABR, Heston) must explicitly enforce butterfly positivity.

📈 Walkthrough Example: Call Spread Arbitrage (Monotonicity Violation)

What to set in the checker:

  • σ(90%): 10%
  • σ(95%): 45% (massive volatility jump)
  • σ(100%): 20%
  • σ(105%): 19%
  • σ(110%): 20%
  • S₀: 100 | T: 1 | r (%): 4

Interpretation of results:

A call option with a higher strike should never cost more than a call option with a lower strike. Here, the massive jump in volatility at the 95% strike makes the $95 call cost approximately $21.73 while the $90 call costs only $13.83. This results in a Call Spread Monotonicity violation.

The Arbitrage Trade: Buy the $90 call and sell the $95 call. You collect approximately $7.90 net premium upfront. At maturity, your portfolio payoff is \(S_T - 90\) (capped at $5 if \(S_T \ge 95\)) or $0 if \(S_T < 90\). Because the payoff is always non-negative, the upfront $7.90 credit represents a pure, risk-free profit.

🦋 Walkthrough Example: Butterfly Arbitrage (Convexity Violation)

What to set in the checker:

  • σ(90%): 28%
  • σ(95%): 15% (volatility dip)
  • σ(100%): 28%
  • σ(105%): 19%
  • σ(110%): 20%
  • S₀: 100 | T: 1 | r (%): 4

Interpretation of results:

The price curve must be convex in the strike price. A butterfly spread (buying a low and a high strike call, and selling two middle calls) must always have a positive cost. Here, the volatility dip at 95% creates a concave bend. The calculations show a negative butterfly cost of approximately -$7.70 (violating Butterfly Positivity).

The Arbitrage Trade: Buy 1 of the $95 call, buy 1 of the $105 call, and sell 2 of the $100 calls. Since the cost of this butterfly is negative, you receive a net credit of approximately $7.70 upfront. At expiry, your maximum loss is $0 (if \(S_T \le 95\) or \(S_T \ge 105\)), and you make additional profit (up to $5) if the stock ends between $95 and $105.

The Option Triplet — Three Views of the Same Market

There is a perfect, one-to-one mathematical relationship between three representations of market information. If you have one, you can derive the other two:

View 1 — Vol Surface

IV plotted vs strike. Shows how expensive "insurance" is. The market's fear map.

📈
View 2 — Option Prices

Dollar prices of calls/puts. Must be convex in K (arbitrage-free condition).

💲
View 3 — Risk-Neutral PDF

The market's probability density over future \(S_T\). The "Holy Grail" — implied probability distribution.

🔮
The Breeden-Litzenberger Formula

The risk-neutral probability density is the second derivative of the call price with respect to the strike:

\[ f^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T = K) = e^{rT}\,\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2} \]
🧠 Masterclass interview answer — "How do you know the market-implied probability of a 20% S&P drop?"
"I look at the 20% OTM put prices across all strikes. By taking the second derivative of the call/put price curve (the Breeden-Litzenberger formula), I extract the Risk-Neutral PDF. The area under the left tail gives the market-implied probability of that drop. This is how event-driven traders price binary risk — earnings, Fed meetings, elections."
What Different Vol Shapes Imply for the Distribution
Flat Vol (BS World)

Perfectly log-normal distribution. Symmetric bell curve. No fat tails. Almost never observed in real markets.

→ Gaussian PDF
Left Skew (Equity)

Distribution is left-shifted and has a fatter left tail. The market puts more probability mass on crashes than rallies. Negative skewness.

→ Negatively skewed PDF
High Curvature (Both Tails)

Fat tails on both sides. Sharp central peak ("leptokurtosis"). Market prices in high probability of staying near current price, but with extreme moves possible.

→ Leptokurtic PDF

Why the Vol Surface Matters — The Strategic Role
1. The Language of the Market

Traders don't quote prices in dollars — those change every second with spot. They quote in "vols." The surface is the standardised coordinate system that remains relatively stable even as the underlying moves.

2. Encoding Market Expectations

Via Breeden-Litzenberger, the surface is the primary tool for extracting the Risk-Neutral Distribution. It tells you the "market odds" on every possible future scenario — the probability of a 10% crash, a squeeze, a range-bound market.

3. The Pricing Anchor — Internal Consistency

Without a unified surface, a trading book would have internal arbitrage. The surface ensures that a 6M option at K=110 is priced logically relative to a 1Y option at K=110. It is the single source of truth for the desk.

4. Bridge to Exotic Products

Exotic options (barriers, Asians, cliquet) don't have observable market prices. You calibrate the surface to liquid vanillas, then use that calibrated surface to price the exotic consistently. Without the surface, structured products cannot be priced.
