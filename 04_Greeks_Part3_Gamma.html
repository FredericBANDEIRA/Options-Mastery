Gamma — The Acceleration of Delta

If Delta is the velocity of an option's price relative to the underlying, Gamma is the acceleration. It tells you how fast your hedge ratio is changing as the market moves.

\[ \Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial S} \]
What it measures
Delta's drift
How much Δ changes for a $1 move in S
Hedger's concern
Re-hedge frequency
High Γ → must rebalance more often to stay Δ-neutral
Sign for long options
Always positive
Long call or long put → Γ > 0. Short option → Γ < 0.
The Convexity Benefit — "God Mode"

The option value (curve) always sits above the linear delta-hedge (straight line) — this gap IS the Gamma P&L

Long Gamma — Positive Convexity

The option outperforms the linear hedge in both directions. Market goes up → Delta increases, you get longer. Market goes down → Delta decreases, you get shorter. You win more on the upside and lose less on the downside.

Short Gamma — Negative Convexity

The mirror image. You underperform in both directions. You lose more when the market moves against you than you gain when it moves with you. Classic "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller."

Three Interview Pressure-Test Questions
"Where is Gamma highest?"

Answer: ATM and close to expiration. As the option approaches expiry, the Delta of an ATM option must jump from 0 to 1 (or 0 to −1) almost instantly. That vertical "kink" in the Delta profile is where Gamma explodes — mathematically, σ√T → 0, so the bell curve N'(d₁) narrows to a spike.

\[\Gamma_{\text{ATM}} = \frac{N'(0)}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}} = \frac{1}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}\sqrt{2\pi}} \xrightarrow{T\to 0} \infty\]
"You're Delta-neutral and long Gamma. Vol explodes. Good or bad?"

Answer: Generally very good. High volatility means large moves in the underlying. Each move generates Gamma P&L = ½Γ(dS)². This P&L scales with the square of the move, so a 2% daily move earns 4× more than a 1% move. As long as the accumulated Gamma P&L exceeds the Theta you're paying, you profit. The key question is whether the realised vol exceeds the implied vol you paid for.

"Can Gamma be negative?" — The classic trap

Answer: For a single option position — only if you are short it. Selling a call or put gives you negative Gamma (concavity). The option value now sits below the linear hedge, meaning you lose more on big moves than you gain on small moves. For a portfolio, Gamma can be positive or negative depending on the net long/short position.

🚩Classic trap: "Gamma is always positive" → only for long positions. Short the option = short Gamma = negative convexity.
"Is Gamma a measure of value?"

Answer: No. Gamma measures the curvature of the payoff — not whether the option is cheap or expensive. You never get Gamma for free: you pay for it via Theta. An option with high Gamma is expensive to hold because the daily time decay is proportionally large. High Gamma is only valuable if the stock moves enough to offset the Theta rent you're paying.

Gamma Hedging vs Delta Hedging
Delta Hedging

Uses the underlying stock (or futures). Eliminates first-order directional risk. Simple linear instrument.

\[\text{Hedge: sell/buy } \Delta \text{ units of } S\]
Gamma Hedging

Requires other options. Cannot hedge Gamma with linear instruments — only convex instruments (options) can offset Gamma. Lower Gamma = less frequent Delta rebalancing needed.

\[\Gamma_{\text{book}} + \Gamma_{\text{hedge option}} = 0\]
🏦 0DTE Gamma Squeeze — Desk Interview Classic:
You're a market maker and everyone is buying 0DTE calls from you. You're short gamma. As the market rallies, your delta becomes increasingly negative. To stay hedged, you must buy the underlying at higher and higher prices. Your buying accelerates the rally → more negative delta → more buying. This feedback loop is a gamma squeeze. Understanding this mechanism is why 0DTE options have dramatically increased intraday volatility in the S&P since 2022.
Gamma under Black-Scholes
The Formula
\[ \Gamma = \frac{N'(d_1)}{S\,\sigma\sqrt{T}} \quad\text{where}\quad N'(x) = \frac{e^{-x^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \]
💡 Key insight: Gamma is essentially the normal PDF N'(d₁) evaluated at the option's d₁, scaled by the inverse of S, σ, and √T. The bell shape of N'(x) explains everything about Gamma's behaviour — it peaks where d₁ = 0 (ATM) and decays to zero in the tails (deep ITM/OTM).
Why Call Gamma = Put Gamma

This follows directly from put-call parity:

\[ C - P = S_0 e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2} - \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial S^2} = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Gamma_C = \Gamma_P \]

A synthetic forward (long call + short put) has a linear payoff — its Delta is constant and its Gamma is zero. Therefore the Gammas of the call and put must be equal and opposite, which means they are identical.

Three Drivers of Gamma Magnitude
1. Moneyness (S/K) — Gamma peaks at ATM

Gamma is highest when d₁ ≈ 0, which occurs when S ≈ K (at-the-money). N'(0) = 1/√(2π) ≈ 0.3989 — the maximum of the normal PDF. As S moves away from K in either direction, d₁ grows in magnitude, N'(d₁) falls toward zero, and Gamma collapses.

2. Time to Expiry (T) — Shorter T → higher ATM Gamma

T appears in the denominator via √T. As T → 0, Gamma → ∞ for ATM options. For deep ITM/OTM, the reverse is true: the outcome is certain, so Gamma actually collapses. This divergence explains why 0DTE options have gamma profiles that look like vertical spikes at the strike.

\[\Gamma_{\text{ATM}} = \frac{1}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}\sqrt{2\pi}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}\]
3. Volatility (σ) — Higher vol lowers ATM Gamma, raises wing Gamma

Higher σ appears in two places: the denominator (directly lowering Gamma) and inside d₁ (widening the distribution). The net effect: higher vol lowers Gamma at the ATM strike (the bell curve flattens) but raises Gamma far from the strike (the tails get fatter). In high-vol regimes, wing options become "alive" with Gamma that wouldn't exist in a low-vol world.

Gamma vs Moneyness — The Bell Curve

Gamma as a function of spot price follows a bell-shaped curve centred near the strike. The shape is directly controlled by the standard normal PDF N'(d₁).

100 20% 1.0 4%
ATM — Peak Gamma

Maximum uncertainty. The option is "on the fence" between expiring ITM or OTM. Delta must change rapidly → maximum Gamma. This is the zone of maximum hedging activity.

Deep OTM — Gamma → 0

Almost certain to expire worthless. A small spot move barely changes the probability of exercise. Delta stays near 0, barely moves → Gamma negligible. The option is "dead."

Deep ITM — Gamma → 0

Almost certain to be exercised. Delta is "locked" near 1 (or −1) and no longer changes with spot. The option behaves like the stock itself → no convexity left → Gamma ≈ 0.

Asymmetry — The Lognormal Drift

The Gamma bell curve is not perfectly centred on the spot price K. Because BS assumes a lognormal distribution, the peak shifts slightly toward the At-The-Money Forward (ATMF):

\[\text{ATMF} = S_0 \cdot e^{(r-q)T} \neq K \text{ unless } r = q\]

This drift becomes more pronounced at longer maturities and higher rates. In high-rate environments (like 2023), the ATMF can be significantly above spot, and practitioners always use ATMF — not spot — as the reference for ATM Gamma.

💡 ATM vs OTM — interview pro tip: ATM options have the highest absolute Gamma. OTM options often have higher Gamma per dollar spent (leverage). For pure vol plays, this matters: OTM options are cheaper but require the stock to travel further to generate Gamma P&L. ATM options are expensive but start generating Gamma P&L immediately from any move.

Short-Dated — The ATM Spike

As T → 0, ATM Gamma → ∞. The bell curve narrows to a spike. Delta must instantly transition from 0 to 1 — causing maximum hedging volatility. This is why 0DTE and weekly options are the most dangerous to be short.

Long-Dated — The Flat Plateau

3-year options have a broad, low Gamma hump. The Delta S-curve is gradual. Hedging is stable and infrequent. Deep ITM/OTM have non-negligible Gamma because there's still significant uncertainty about the terminal outcome.

Negative Gamma Near Maturity — The Most Dangerous Trade

The Trader's Dilemma
The Three Problems
  • Speed requirement: Delta changes violently with every tick. You must rebalance faster than ever.
  • Slippage trap: Frequent rebalancing at wider spreads can cost more than the entire Theta earned.
  • Uncertainty: You don't know if you'll be assigned or not — and you might not find out until after close.
Two Clean Exits
  1. Fully hedge at once: Accept the Delta immediately. Stop the constant rebalancing bleed.
  2. Fully unwind: Buy back the option. Remove the Gamma risk entirely. Cleanest solution — often the right one despite the cost.
📍 Pin Risk in full detail: The stock closes at exactly K = $100.00 on expiry. You're short 100 calls. You don't know if you'll be assigned. Options settle after market close.

Scenario A — stock closes at $99.99: You hedge for 0 assignment, stock gaps to $100.50 on Monday open. You're long 10,000 shares with no offset.
Scenario B — stock closes at $100.01: You hedge for full assignment, stock gaps to $99.50 on Monday open. You're short 10,000 shares delta-hedged against nothing.

This is why many desks always unwind short options in the final hour of their expiry, accepting the spread cost as cheap insurance.
Gamma P&L — The Core Formula
Taylor Expansion — Average Delta Logic
\[ p_1 = p_0 + \underbrace{\Delta_0\, dS}_{\text{hedged out}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\,\Gamma_0\,(dS)^2}_{\textbf{Gamma P\&L}} \]
⚡ The magic of (dS)²: The price change is squared. For a long Gamma position (Γ > 0), this term is always positive regardless of whether the stock goes up or down. You make money on every move. You only lose if the stock doesn't move enough to offset Theta.
Average Delta — The Derivation

Delta is not constant during a move. It drifts from Δ₀ to Δ₁ = Δ₀ + Γ·dS. The option price change uses the average delta over the move:

\[ \Delta_1 = \Delta_0 + \Gamma_0\,dS \qquad \Delta_{\text{avg}} = \Delta_0 + \frac{\Gamma_0\,dS}{2} \] \[ p_1 = p_0 + \Delta_{\text{avg}}\cdot dS = p_0 + \Delta_0\,dS + \frac{\Gamma_0\,(dS)^2}{2} \]
Long option P&L
\(\Delta_0 dS + \frac{\Gamma_0(dS)^2}{2}\)
Short hedge P&L
\(-\Delta_0 dS\)
Net: Δ₀·dS + ½Γ₀(dS)² − Δ₀·dS = ½Γ₀(dS)²
Gamma vs Theta — The Core Trade-off
Gamma Income

Earned by continuously rebalancing Delta. Every time the underlying moves, you "scalp" the convexity. The larger the move, the more P&L (scales with dS²).

\[\text{Gamma P\&L} = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2 \sigma_R^2\,dt\]
Theta Cost ("Rent")

The daily "rent" for holding the option. Negative for all long option positions. Paid every day regardless of whether the stock moves. Certain cost vs uncertain gain.

\[\text{Break-even: } \frac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2 \sigma_R^2 = |\Theta|\,dt\]

🔑 The Gamma Rent analogy (perfect interview answer):
Being long Gamma is like paying rent (Theta) to occupy a house at the Strike. If the storm (Volatility) hits while you're in the house, you collect insurance (Gamma P&L). If the storm hits while the stock is miles away from your strike, you paid rent for nothing. Volatility capture is about efficiency — not just the total realised vol number.
The Paradox — RV > IV but Still Lose Money

It is possible to buy an option at IV = 15% and have the stock realise 20% — yet still lose money.

Example: Buy 1-month call at 15% IV. Stock is dead flat for 29 days (bleeding Theta every day). On Day 30, the stock gaps 10% in one hour. Total monthly RV = 20% > 15%. But you likely lost money because the Gamma P&L on that final day (when the option was nearly expired) couldn't offset 29 days of Theta bleed.
⚠️Lesson: Gamma P&L = ½ × Γ × (dS)² × (local Gamma at the time). If vol occurs when you're deep OTM or near expiry, Gamma is low and you earn little. Timing and location of vol matter as much as total RV.
Dollar Gamma — Risk Management Grade

Standard Gamma (Γ) is hard to use in risk management across different underlyings. A stock at $10 and a stock at $1,000 have very different Gammas even if their risk profiles are similar. Dollar Gamma normalises this.

The Problem with Standard Gamma
Problem 1: Unit Dependency

Gamma is measured in "delta change per $1 move." A $1 move on a $10 stock is 10%. The same $1 move on a $1,000 stock is 0.1%. Raw Gamma is not comparable across different price levels.

Problem 2: Inverse Price Relationship

The BS formula has S in the denominator: Γ = N'(d₁)/(Sσ√T). Higher stock price → lower Gamma, mechanically. This makes it impossible to compare convexity across a multi-asset book without normalisation.

Problem 3: Not Additive

Summing raw Gammas across a portfolio is meaningless if the positions have different S levels. Dollar Gamma provides an additive risk measure across the entire book.

Dollar Delta & Dollar Gamma — The Solution
Dollar Delta (Δ$)
\[\$\Delta = \Delta \times S\]

Cash equivalent directional exposure. "I have $50,000 of long Delta" is more meaningful than "I have Δ = 0.5 on a $100k notional." It directly tells you how much equivalent stock you're holding.

Dollar Gamma ($Γ)
\[\$\Gamma = \frac{\Gamma \times S^2}{100}\]

Change in Dollar Delta for a 1% move in the underlying. If you have $100k Dollar Gamma and the stock moves 1%, your Dollar Delta changes by $100k. Immediately intuitive for risk managers.

The Interview-Ready P&L Formula

Substituting the Dollar Gamma definition into the standard Gamma P&L formula:

\[ \text{Gamma P\&L} = 50 \times \$\Gamma \times R^2 \]

where R is the percentage return (e.g., R = 0.03 for a 3% move). No need to know S or Γ separately — just Dollar Gamma and the % move.

⚡ Example: Long $100,000 Dollar Gamma. Market moves 3% (R = 0.03):
P&L = 50 × $100,000 × 0.03² = 50 × 100,000 × 0.0009 = $4,500
Market moves 6% (R = 0.06): P&L = 50 × 100,000 × 0.0036 = $18,000
Doubling the move quadruples the P&L — the square law in action.
