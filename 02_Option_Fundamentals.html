01 — Arithmetic vs Geometric Returns

The distinction between arithmetic and geometric returns is foundational for options because it explains why volatility has a cost — the central idea behind option pricing.

Arithmetic Mean
\(\mu_a = \frac{1}{n}\sum r_i\)
Simple average — what you'd get if returns were additive
Geometric Mean (CAGR)
\(\mu_g = \left(\prod(1+r_i)\right)^{1/n}-1\)
Actual compounded growth rate — what you actually earn
02 — The Volatility Drag
The Core Formula
\[ \mu_g \approx \mu_a - \frac{\sigma^2}{2} \]

Compounding is multiplicative, not additive. A loss requires a proportionally larger gain to recover — the math is asymmetric:

−10% loss
Need +11.1% to break even
−25% loss
Need +33.3% to break even
−50% loss
Need +100% to break even
🎯 Interview answer — "Why do we care about vol if E[r] > 0?"
"Because we don't eat arithmetic returns — we eat geometric returns. Volatility drag ensures that for the same expected return, a higher-vol strategy produces lower terminal wealth with certainty. The σ²/2 penalty grows quadratically with volatility."

03 — Log-Normal Distribution

Stock prices follow a log-normal distribution because log-returns (continuously compounded) are normally distributed. This is a core assumption in Black-Scholes.

Why Log-Normal (not Normal)?
  • Prices can't go below zero — log-normal has a positive support
  • Returns compound multiplicatively: \(S_T = S_0 \cdot e^{\mu T + \sigma W_T}\)
  • Log-returns \(\ln(S_T/S_0)\) are normally distributed
  • The distribution is right-skewed — large gains are possible, losses are bounded at −100%
Key Parameters
\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{S_T}{S_0}\right) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)T,\; \sigma^2 T\right) \]

Note the \(-\sigma^2/2\) term — this is the volatility drag appearing directly in the log-normal mean. The actual drift of log-prices is reduced by the variance penalty.

04 — Risk-Neutral Pricing

One of the most counterintuitive ideas in finance: to price derivatives, we don't need the real-world expected return of the stock. Instead we price under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\).

Real-World Measure ℙ
Stocks earn \(\mu > r\)
Investors demand a risk premium. μ is unknown and hard to estimate.
Risk-Neutral Measure ℚ
Everything earns \(r\)
We price as if investors were risk-neutral. Only r matters for option pricing.
\[ V_0 = e^{-rT}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\text{Payoff}_T] \]
💡 The insight: Because we can dynamically hedge an option using the underlying, the risk premium cancels out. The only arbitrage-free price is the discounted expected payoff under ℚ. This is the fundamental theorem of asset pricing — and the real foundation of Black-Scholes.
🚩 Common misconception: "The option price depends on where I think the stock will go." — Wrong. The option price only depends on σ, S, K, r, T. Your view on the stock's direction doesn't change the no-arbitrage price. What matters is your view on volatility.
Options — Definition & Core Notation

An option gives its holder a right (not an obligation) to buy or sell an underlying asset at a pre-agreed price. The seller (writer) takes on the corresponding obligation.

Short (Writer/Seller)
OBLIGATION
Receives premium upfront. Must perform if exercised. Max gain = premium.

European vs American Options
European

Can only be exercised at maturity T. Cleaner to price analytically (Black-Scholes). Traded on most equity index options (e.g. SPX, CAC 40 options).

American

Can be exercised any time before T. Worth at least as much as European. Early exercise can be optimal for puts on near-zero stocks or calls on high-dividend stocks.

Moneyness

Moneyness describes the relationship between S (current spot) and K (strike). It determines the intrinsic value and the probability of exercise.

MoneynessCall ConditionPut ConditionIntrinsic Value
ITM In-The-Money \(S > K\)\(S < K\) Positive — option has immediate exercise value
ATM At-The-Money \(S = K\)\(S = K\) Zero — spot equals strike
OTM Out-of-The-Money \(S < K\)\(S > K\) Zero — only time value remains
💡 ATM Spot vs. ATM Forward:
Moneyness can be measured relative to either the current spot price or the forward price:
  • ATM Spot (ATMS): Comparing the fixed strike \(K\) directly to the current spot price \(S_t\). The option is ATM Spot when \(K = S_t\) (with no interest rate or time factors).
  • ATM Forward (ATMF): Comparing the fixed strike \(K\) to the forward price \(F_t = S_t e^{r(T-t)}\) (today's spot price forwarded to the expiration date). The option is ATM Forward when \(K = F_t\).
Why this distinction matters:
If interest rates are positive (\(r > 0\)) and there is time remaining (\(T-t > 0\)), the forward price \(F_t\) is higher than the spot price \(S_t\).
If you hold a call option with a strike \(K = 100\) when the stock trades at \(S_t = 100\), the option is ATM Spot. However, because the stock is expected to drift upwards over time due to interest rates, under risk-neutral pricing the stock is expected to end up above 100. To have a truly "neutral" option centered in the probability distribution at maturity, the strike would need to match the ATM Forward price (e.g., \(K = 105\)).
The Four Vanilla Payoffs
📈 Long Call
\(\text{Payoff} = \max(S_T - K,\; 0)\)

Pay the premium upfront, receive upside above K. Right to buy.

Max Loss: Premium Upside: Unlimited
Breakeven: \(K + \text{Premium}\)
📉 Short Call
\(\text{Payoff} = -\max(S_T - K,\; 0)\)

Collect premium, bear unlimited upside risk if S rises. Obligation to sell.

Max Gain: Premium Loss: Unlimited
Breakeven: \(K + \text{Premium}\)
🛡️ Long Put
\(\text{Payoff} = \max(K - S_T,\; 0)\)

Pay premium, profit when S falls below K. Right to sell. Portfolio insurance.

Max Loss: Premium Profit if S → 0
Breakeven: \(K - \text{Premium}\)
⚡ Short Put
\(\text{Payoff} = -\max(K - S_T,\; 0)\)

Collect premium, obligated to buy at K if S crashes. Obligation to buy.

Max Gain: Premium Max Loss: K − Prem
Breakeven: \(K - \text{Premium}\)

Profit vs Payoff — Don't Confuse Them
Payoff (at maturity)
\[\text{Payoff} = \max(S_T - K,\; 0)\]

The gross cash flow at expiry. Does not account for what you paid to enter the trade.

Profit (P&L)
\[\text{Profit} = \text{Payoff} - \text{Premium} \cdot e^{rT}\]

The net gain/loss, including the future value of the premium paid. This is what matters for performance.

Black-Scholes — Intuitive Decomposition
CALL PRICE = ASSET PART − CASH PART
\(S \cdot N(d_1)\)
−
\(K e^{-rT} \cdot N(d_2)\)
ASSET PART
"What you receive"
δ-hedged stock position
CASH PART
"What you pay"
PV of strike × prob. exercise
Asset Part — S·N(d₁)

You are synthetically long the stock. Since you don't own the full share, you hold a fraction equal to Delta = N(d₁). Delta is how many shares of stock you need to perfectly hedge the option.

\[N(d_1) = \Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S}\]
Cash Part — Ke^(-rT)·N(d₂)

You are short a bond (borrowed money to buy the stock). The amount owed is the present value of the strike K, but only paid with probability N(d₂) — the risk-neutral probability the option expires ITM.

\[N(d_2) = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)\]
d₁ and d₂ — Full Formulas
d₁ — The Replicating Component
\[ d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} \]

A Z-score measuring distance to strike, adjusted upward by σ²/2. N(d₁) = Delta — the hedge ratio, the fraction of a share to hold.

d₂ — The Probability Component
\[ d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T} = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} \]

The Z-score without the vol adjustment. N(d₂) = risk-neutral probability of ending ITM. Note the −σ²/2 vs +σ²/2 difference — this is the volatility drag again!

💡 Why d₁ > d₂ always? Because of the log-normal distribution's right skew — high vol creates a fatter right tail. The stock needs a higher "push" to stay ITM (measured by d₁/Delta) than the pure probability of finishing ITM (d₂). The gap is exactly σ√T — proportional to how much volatility drag matters over the horizon.
Put Formula
Put via Black-Scholes
\[ P = K e^{-rT} \cdot N(-d_2) - S \cdot N(-d_1) \]
Put via Put-Call Parity (shortcut)
\[ P = C - S + K e^{-rT} \]
🔑 In practice, traders derive the put price from the call price via put-call parity — they don't re-run BS separately. This is faster and avoids rounding errors.

The Replication Argument — Why BS Works

Black-Scholes is really just the cost of a dynamic hedging strategy:

Step 1
Sell the option, collect C
You're now short gamma — dangerous if stock moves
Step 2
Buy Δ = N(d₁) shares
Delta-hedge: neutralise directional exposure instantly
Step 3 (continuous)
Rebalance Δ daily
As S moves, Δ changes — you buy high and sell low to stay hedged. This cost IS the option premium.
⚠️ The real-world complication: Continuous rebalancing is impossible. Discrete hedging introduces residual risk ("gamma P&L"). The actual cost of hedging is path-dependent — realised vol determines whether the hedge makes or loses money vs the premium collected. This is why traders obsess over realised vs implied vol.
The ATM Rule-of-Thumb — A Trader's Mental Math
The Formula
\[ C_{\text{ATM}} \approx 0.4 \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{T} \cdot S_0 \]
⚡ Where does 0.4 come from?
When S = K and r ≈ 0, BS simplifies to \(C \approx S \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\). Since \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx 0.3989\), traders round to 0.4. This is the height of the standard normal density PDF at zero.
Show Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation

To derive the approximation, we start with the standard Black-Scholes Call formula and assume ATM Spot (\(S = K\)) and zero interest rates (\(r = 0\)):

  1. Simplify the Black-Scholes Formula:
    Since \(r = 0\), the discount factor \(e^{-rT} = 1\). With \(S = K\), the equation reduces to: \[ C = S \cdot \left[ N(d_1) - N(d_2) \right] \]
  2. Simplify \(d_1\) and \(d_2\):
    Since \(S = K\), the term \(\ln(S/K) = 0\). The formula for \(d_1\) becomes: \[ d_1 = \frac{\frac{\sigma^2}{2}T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} = \frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T} \] Since \(d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}\), we get: \[ d_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T} \]
  3. First-Order Taylor Expansion of \(N(x)\):
    For short-dated options, the variance term \(\sigma\sqrt{T}\) is small. We can approximate the cumulative normal CDF \(N(x)\) around \(x = 0\) using its tangent line: \[ N(x) \approx N(0) + N'(0)\cdot x \] Where:
    • \(N(0) = 0.5\) (50% probability at the center).
    • \(N'(0) = n(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx 0.3989\) (the height of the standard normal density at 0).
    So, \(N(x) \approx 0.5 + 0.3989 \cdot x\).
  4. Substitute \(d_1\) and \(d_2\) into the approximation: \[ N(d_1) - N(d_2) \approx \left[ 0.5 + 0.3989\left(\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T}\right) \right] - \left[ 0.5 - 0.3989\left(\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T}\right) \right] \] Simplifying this yields: \[ N(d_1) - N(d_2) \approx 2 \cdot 0.3989 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T}\right) = 0.3989 \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T} \]
  5. Final Substitution: \[ C \approx S \cdot 0.3989 \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T} \approx 0.4 \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T} \cdot S \]
What This Formula Tells You
Vol Linearity

Option price is directly proportional to σ. Double the vol → double the price. This is only true ATM — OTM options have a non-linear vol relationship (the smile).

√T Scaling

Time value grows with √T, not T. A 4-year option costs twice a 1-year option, not four times. Theta (time decay) is not linear — it accelerates near expiry.

Straddle = 2×

Since ATM call ≈ ATM put (by put-call parity when r ≈ 0), a straddle costs ≈ 0.8·σ√T·S. This is the market's "implied move" — what the market prices in as the expected move.

The Straddle as a "Volatility Price Tag"
Implied Move
\[ \text{Expected Move} \approx 0.8 \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{T} \cdot S_0 \]

The straddle price is what the market is implying the stock will move by, on average. This is how traders "read" the vol surface — the straddle price is the market's implied move.

Daily Move Estimate
\[ \text{Daily Move} \approx \sigma \cdot S_0 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{252}} \approx \frac{\sigma \cdot S_0}{16} \]

Quick rule: divide annual vol by 16 (≈√252) to get the expected daily move in dollar terms. At 20% vol on a $100 stock → $1.25/day expected move.

No-Arbitrage Price Boundaries

These bounds are enforced by pure arbitrage logic — no model assumptions needed. Violating them creates a risk-free profit.

Option Upper Bound Lower Bound (European) Lower Bound (American)
Call \(S_t\) \(\max\!\left(0,\; S_t - Ke^{-rT}\right)\) \(\max(0,\; S_t - K)\)
Put \(Ke^{-rT}\) (Eur) / \(K\) (Am) \(\max\!\left(0,\; Ke^{-rT} - S_t\right)\) \(\max(0,\; K - S_t)\)
Intuition for Each Bound
Call Upper Bound: C ≤ S

A call is the right to buy the stock. It can never be worth more than the stock itself — the stock is the best-case scenario for the call holder (if K = 0, the call IS the stock). If C > S, sell the call and buy the stock: instant risk-free profit.

European Call Lower Bound: C ≥ S − Ke^(−rT)

You don't pay K until maturity T. So the effective cost of exercising is only the present value of K, not K itself. The European lower bound accounts for the time value of the cash you defer paying.

Arbitrage if violated: If \(C < S - Ke^{-rT}\), then: (1) buy the call, (2) short the stock, (3) invest PV(K) at r. At T: use proceeds to deliver stock via call exercise → guaranteed profit.

\[C \geq S_t - K e^{-rT} \geq 0\]
Put Upper Bound: P ≤ Ke^(−rT) [European] / P ≤ K [American]

European: The best outcome is that \(S_T = 0\), giving a payoff of \(K\) received at \(T\). Discounted: \(\text{PV} = K e^{-rT}\).

American: Since you can exercise immediately, you could receive \(K\) right now — so the American put's max value is the full \(K\) (not PV of \(K\)). This is why American puts are sometimes worth more than European puts.

Early Exercise — When Does It Make Sense?
American Call — Almost Never Early Exercise

For non-dividend stocks: the European lower bound \(S - Ke^{-rT}\) > the American lower bound \(S - K\) because \(Ke^{-rT} < K\). You "get more" by holding — the money you haven't paid yet earns interest. Better to sell the call than exercise early.

Exception: Deep ITM call on high-dividend stock just before ex-date. The dividend makes early exercise worthwhile if its PV exceeds the lost optionality.

American Put — Sometimes Early Exercise

If the stock approaches zero, receiving K now is better than receiving K at T (time value of money). There's also no downside optionality left (S can't go below 0).

The intuition: for a put, you want to exercise when the interest earned on the strike proceeds (r × K × T) exceeds the remaining time value of the put. Deep ITM puts on near-zero stocks → exercise immediately.

💡 Summary rule: Never early-exercise an American call on a non-dividend stock. May early-exercise an American put when deeply ITM. This asymmetry is why American calls and European calls have the same price on non-dividend stocks, but American puts are worth more than European puts.

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value
\[ \underbrace{C}_{\text{Premium}} = \underbrace{\max(S - K,\; 0)}_{\text{Intrinsic Value}} + \underbrace{\text{TV}}_{\text{Time Value}} \]
Intrinsic Value

The value if exercised right now. The "real money" component. Only ITM options have positive intrinsic value.

\[ IV_{\text{call}} = \max(S - K,\; 0) \] \[ IV_{\text{put}} = \max(K - S,\; 0) \]

ATM and OTM options: IV = 0. All premium is time value.

Time Value (Extrinsic Value)

The premium above intrinsic value. It reflects optionality — the possibility that the option moves further ITM before expiry. Time value is maximised ATM.

\[ \text{TV} = C - \max(S - K,\; 0) \]

TV → 0 as T → 0. This decay is Theta (Θ) — the daily "rent" you pay for holding an option.

Key Drivers of Time Value
DriverEffect on TVIntuition
Volatility (σ)↑ σ → ↑ TVMore chance of big moves into the money. Vol is essentially the price of time value.
Time to Expiry (T)↑ T → ↑ TVMore time = more opportunities for the stock to move ITM. TV → 0 at expiry.
MoneynessATM maximises TVDeep ITM/OTM options have low TV. ATM has maximum uncertainty → maximum optionality value.
Risk-Free Rate (r)↑ r → ↑ Call TV, ↓ Put TVHigher r → lower PV of strike → call cheaper to finance, put less attractive to exercise early.
Dividends (q)↑ q → ↓ Call TV, ↑ Put TVDividends reduce S (ex-date drop) → less upside for call, more downside protection value for put.
Theta — The Cost of Holding Time
Time Decay Formula (Approximation)
\[ \Theta \approx -\frac{S \cdot \sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx -\frac{C_{\text{ATM}}}{2T} \]

Theta is negative for option buyers — time decay is a cost. The ATM option loses roughly half its value in the last quarter of its life. Theta is not constant — it accelerates as expiry approaches.

Practical Rule
Theta ≈ \(−C_{ATM}\) / (2T)
For a $10 ATM option with T=1 year, Theta ≈ −$5/year = −$0.02/day
🚩 Gamma vs Theta trade-off: Long options are long gamma (you profit from large moves) and short theta (you pay daily decay). Short options are short gamma and long theta. This is the central tension in options trading: you're always selling convexity to someone, or buying it from them.

Put-Call Parity
The Fundamental Relationship
\[ C - P = S_0 - K e^{-rT} \]

Or equivalently, the portfolio identity:

\[ C + K e^{-rT} = P + S_0 \]
Proof by No-Arbitrage (Two Portfolios)
Portfolio A — "Insured Stock"

Long the stock + Long a put (strike \(K\))
Payoff at \(T\): \(\max(S_T, K)\)

  • If \(S_T > K\): worth \(S_T\) (put expires worthless)
  • If \(S_T < K\): worth \(K\) (put pays \(K - S_T\))
Portfolio B — "Cash + Call"

Long a call (strike \(K\)) + Invest \(K e^{-rT}\) in bonds
Payoff at \(T\): \(\max(S_T, K)\)

  • If \(S_T > K\): call pays \(S_T - K\), bond pays \(K\) → total \(S_T\)
  • If \(S_T < K\): call worthless, bond pays \(K\) → total \(K\)
🔑 Both portfolios have identical payoffs in every scenario → they must cost the same today: \(P + S_0 = C + K e^{-rT}\). This requires no model assumptions — it is a pure no-arbitrage result.
What Put-Call Parity Implies
RearrangementInterpretation
\(C = P + S_0 - Ke^{-rT}\) Call = Put + Long Stock + Borrowing PV(K)
\(P = C - S_0 + Ke^{-rT}\) Put = Call + Short Stock + Lending PV(K)
\(C - P = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}\) Standard Put-Call Parity (expressed in terms of spot price \(S_0\))
\(C - P = (F_0 - K)e^{-rT}\) Synthetic Forward: Long call + Short put at strike \(K\) is identical to a forward contract at strike \(K\)
💡 The forward connection: A long call + short put at the same strike and maturity is identical to a forward contract to buy the stock at K. This is the key link between the derivatives tabs:
Forward price = \(S_0 e^{rT}\) → set K = F₀ → C = P (for ATMF options). That's why ATMF calls and puts have the same price.

Cost of Hedging — The Dealer's Perspective

The option's price should reflect the cost of dynamically hedging it. From the dealer's viewpoint:

Selling a Call
  • Must buy Δ shares of stock to hedge (delta hedge)
  • As stock rises, buy more shares (buy high)
  • As stock falls, sell shares (sell low)
  • The net cost of this rebalancing = the option premium
Realised vs Implied Vol
  • Premium charged reflects implied vol (IV)
  • Actual hedge cost depends on realised vol (RV)
  • P&L of delta-hedger = ½ × Gamma × S² × (RV² − IV²) × dt
  • If RV > IV → hedging costs more than premium → loss
  • If RV < IV → hedging costs less than premium → profit
🎯 S&T interview perspective: "Selling options is selling volatility. Your P&L depends not on where the stock ends up, but on whether the stock moved more or less than the vol implied in the price you sold. You make money if the market overpriced vol (IV > RV). You lose if you underpriced it."
