Delta under Black-Scholes
Call Delta
\[\Delta_C = N(d_1)\]
Put Delta
\[\Delta_P = N(d_1) - 1\]
\[ d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} \]
💡 Put-Call Parity check: Δ_C − Δ_P = N(d₁) − (N(d₁) − 1) = 1. This equals the delta of a long forward — consistent with C − P = Forward, which is exactly put-call parity. The relationship is self-consistent.
Three Drivers of Delta
1. Moneyness (S/K) — The Dominant Driver

As spot rises relative to strike, d₁ increases, and N(d₁) → 1. The call becomes more likely to expire ITM, so you need more shares to hedge it. As S falls, d₁ → −∞, N(d₁) → 0, call delta → 0.

\[\frac{S}{K} \uparrow \implies d_1 \uparrow \implies N(d_1) \uparrow \implies \Delta_C \uparrow\]
2. Volatility (σ) — The "Flattening" Effect

Higher vol spreads the probability distribution wider. OTM options become more likely to end ITM (their delta rises toward 0.5). ITM options might swing back to OTM (their delta falls from 1 toward 0.5). High vol compresses all deltas toward 0.5.

\[\sigma \uparrow \implies \text{OTM } \Delta \uparrow,\; \text{ITM } \Delta \downarrow \implies \text{all } \Delta \to 0.5\]

This is the Vanna effect — delta changes when vol changes, even if spot doesn't move.

3. Time to Expiry (T) — The "Steepening" Effect

As T → 0 (expiry approaches), the delta S-curve becomes steeper. With one day to go, a stock at K+$1 has Δ ≈ 1 and at K−$1 has Δ ≈ 0. The ATM delta remains ≈ 0.5 but the transition becomes nearly a step function. This is why pin risk is so dangerous at expiry.

\[T \to 0 \implies \Delta \to \mathbf{1}_{S > K} \quad \text{(Heaviside step function at expiry)}\]

Additional Factors (Beyond the BS Formula)
Dividends (q)

Dividends reduce the forward price. Higher q lowers call delta and raises put delta. For dividend futures, the forward (not spot) is the relevant price process.

\[\Delta_C^{\text{div}} = e^{-qT}N(d_1)\]
Interest Rates (r)

Higher r increases the forward (cost of carry). Raises call delta slightly, lowers put delta. For short-dated equity options, this effect is usually tiny — but it matters for long-dated or high-rate environments.

Liquidity / Slippage

In illiquid markets, the theoretical delta may differ from the executable hedge ratio. Bid-ask spreads on the underlying widen the cost of staying delta-neutral, making the optimal hedge frequency a function of transaction costs.

How Delta Changes — Charm & Vanna
Charm — Impact of Time on Delta
Charm Definition
\[\text{Charm} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S\,\partial t} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial t}\]

Even if the stock doesn't move, your hedge ratio changes as time passes. This is the Charm effect — delta bleed. Managing it means re-hedging even on flat days.

Steepening Curve

As T → 0, the delta S-curve becomes dramatically steeper around the strike. The longer the curve (3Y), the flatter and more stable. The hedge ratio barely changes as spot moves far from the strike.

⚠️ Pin Risk

On expiry day (1-day line), the delta is near 0 or 1 with an almost vertical transition at K. A $0.01 move in the stock forces a complete hedge flip from 0% to 100% of shares. This is "pin risk" — the nightmare scenario for market makers.

📍 Pin risk in practice (interview story): You're short a call struck at 100, expiry tomorrow. At 3pm the stock is at 99.95. Your delta is 0.05 (near-zero hedge). At 4pm the stock prints 100.05. Your delta is now 0.95 — you need to instantly buy nearly the full notional in shares. That buying pressure can push the stock further through the strike, triggering more buying. This feedback loop is "pinning" — the stock literally gets pinned at the strike by options market makers desperately hedging.
Vanna — Impact of Volatility on Delta
Vanna Definition
\[\text{Vanna} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S\,\partial\sigma} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial S}\]

Vanna measures how delta changes when vol changes (or equivalently, how vega changes when spot changes). It's the key Greek for managing the volatility smile's impact on your hedge ratio.

100 1.0 4%
Low Vol → Step Function

At low IV (10%), the market is certain about outcomes. Delta is near 0 or 1 with a sharp transition. There is almost no "fuzz" — the option is clearly ITM or OTM.

High Vol → Flat S-Curve

At high IV (80%), uncertainty spreads. OTM deltas rise (could still swing ITM), ITM deltas fall (could swing back OTM). Every option delta is compressed toward 0.5. This is the Vanna effect.

🧠 Vanna for risk managers (VIX spike scenario): You're delta-hedged and the VIX spikes 10 points. Your book's delta changes — not because spot moved, but because vol moved. If you're long vanna, your call deltas increased, so you now need to buy more shares even though the stock went nowhere. This forced buying from vol-driven delta changes is a significant source of short-term momentum in crash scenarios.
Delta ≠ P(ITM) — A Critical Distinction

One of the most common confusions in options. Delta and the probability of expiring ITM are related but fundamentally different:

\[ \Delta_C = N(d_1) \qquad\qquad P(\text{ITM}) = N(d_2) = N(d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}) \]
🚩 Interview trap: "An ATM call has delta 0.5 — so there's a 50% chance it finishes ITM?" — Not quite. N(d₂) is the risk-neutral P(ITM). For ATM options, d₁ ≈ d₂ ≈ 0 and N(0) = 0.5, so the approximation works. But for OTM options or high-vol environments, the gap between N(d₁) and N(d₂) = σ√T becomes significant.
Why They Diverge — The Skewness Effect

The extreme example from your notes perfectly illustrates this:

Scenario
Stock at €10 — binary outcome tomorrow
10% chance: goes to €100  |  90% chance: goes to €0
50-strike call
Price = €5 → Δ = 0.5
P(ITM) = only 10%! Delta is 5× the actual probability.
⚡ The math: The option costs €5 = 10% × (€100 − €50) = 10% × €50. Delta = ∂C/∂S. A $1 move in the stock shifts the €10 price by enough to change the call value by ~$0.5. Hence Δ = 0.5. But P(ITM) = 10%. In a positively skewed distribution (rare but large upmoves), delta diverges massively from P(ITM).
The BS Relationship: N(d₁) vs N(d₂)
\[d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T} \implies N(d_2) < N(d_1) \text{ always (since } \sigma\sqrt{T} > 0\text{)}\]
N(d₁) = Delta

The hedge ratio — how many shares to hold. Adjusted upward by σ²/2 in d₁ to account for the option's convexity and the lognormal distribution's right skew. Always ≥ N(d₂).

N(d₂) = Risk-Neutral P(ITM)

The probability the option finishes ITM under the risk-neutral measure ℚ. Lower than delta because it does NOT account for the "size of the win" when it does finish ITM — it just counts the probability.

🔑 When does it matter most? The gap N(d₁) − N(d₂) = σ√T. For a 1Y option at 20% vol: gap ≈ 0.20. For a 2Y at 40% vol: gap ≈ 0.57. For deep OTM options and high-vol environments, using delta as a probability proxy can be massively misleading. Always use N(d₂) when you want a probability; always use N(d₁) when you want a hedge ratio.
Delta Hedging P&L — The Game of Volatility Arbitrage

A trader sells a call, collects the premium (priced at IV), and delta-hedges daily. In BS theory, the hedge perfectly replicates the option and P&L = 0. In practice, there are two sources of deviation:

Source 1: Discrete Hedging
Tracking Error
BS assumes continuous hedging. Daily rebalancing introduces error between re-hedges. The faster the market moves between hedges, the larger the mismatch.
Source 2: RV vs IV
Vol Arbitrage P&L
The core driver. If stock moves less than priced in (RV < IV), the short option makes money. If it moves more (RV > IV), it loses. This is a pure vol bet.
The Daily P&L Formula
Delta-Hedged P&L per time step dt
\[ \text{P\&L} = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2\!\left(\sigma_R^2 - \sigma_{IV}^2\right)dt \]
If RV < IV
Profit ✓
Stock moved less than priced. Short option wins. The hedge cost less than the premium.
If RV = IV
Break-even
Hedge costs exactly match option premium received. Zero edge.
If RV > IV
Loss ✗
Stock moved more than priced. Short option loses. The hedge cost more than the premium received.
🔑 The key insight: After delta-hedging, your P&L depends not on the direction the stock moves — only on the magnitude of daily moves vs the IV you sold. You've converted a directional bet into a pure volatility bet. This is the foundational principle of volatility trading.

The IV Drop Scenario

You sell a call at IV = 25%. Immediately after, IV drops to 5%.

Immediate paper profit (Vega P&L)

The option's mark-to-market value collapses because IV dropped. You see a nice profit in your book. This is your Vega P&L: ΔP&L ≈ ν × Δσ = ν × (−20%). Looks great on the screen.

But the final P&L depends on realised vol

If you hold to maturity and keep delta-hedging, that paper profit may evaporate. What ultimately matters is: did the stock realise 25% vol or 5% vol over the option's life? The Vega gain is only locked in if you buy back the option in the market at 5% IV.

⚠️ The rule: To lock in a Vega gain, you must close the position. Continuing to delta-hedge means your P&L reverts to depending on realised vol — not the current mark. Paper profits from vol moves must be monetised to be real profits.
