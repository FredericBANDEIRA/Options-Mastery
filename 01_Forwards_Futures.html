What is a Forward Contract?

A forward contract is a bilateral, OTC agreement to buy or sell an underlying asset at a pre-agreed price (the forward price F₀) on a specified future date T. No money changes hands at inception — the contract has zero initial value by construction (the forward price is set so that V₀ = 0).

Key Parties
Long (Buyer)

Agrees to buy the asset at F₀. Profits when \(S_T > F_0\). Classic use: importer hedging FX, or speculator expecting price rise.

Short (Seller)

Agrees to sell the asset at F₀. Profits when \(S_T < F_0\). Classic use: exporter hedging receivables, producer hedging output price.

No-Arbitrage Pricing — Cost of Carry

The forward price is derived by asking: "What does it cost to synthetically replicate owning the asset at time T?". You borrow cash today, buy the spot asset, and carry it to maturity. The no-arbitrage forward price equals the cost of that carry strategy.

⚠️ Core insight: The forward price is NOT a forecast of the future spot price. It is the no-arbitrage price that makes both sides of the contract equally attractive at inception.
The Four Canonical Formulas
1. No Income Asset (e.g. non-dividend stock, gold)
\[ F_0 = S_0 \cdot e^{rT} \]

You borrow S₀ at the risk-free rate r, buy the asset, and carry it for T years. At maturity you owe \(S_0 e^{rT}\) — that's what the buyer must pay.

SymbolMeaning
S₀Current spot price
rContinuously-compounded risk-free rate
TTime to maturity (in years)
2. Known Discrete Cash Income (e.g. dividend-paying stock, coupon bond)
\[ F_0 = (S_0 - I) \cdot e^{rT} \]

Where \(I\) is the present value of all cash flows (dividends, coupons) received during the life of the forward. The long does NOT receive those cash flows — they reduce what you effectively pay to carry the asset.

💡 Think of it as: you only need to borrow \(S_0 - I\) because the income stream "offsets" part of your financing cost.
3. Continuous Dividend Yield (e.g. equity index, FX)
\[ F_0 = S_0 \cdot e^{(r - q)T} \]

\(q\) is the continuous dividend yield. Used for equity indices (S&P 500 futures) and currencies (where the foreign risk-free rate plays the role of \(q\) — Interest Rate Parity).

FX Forward — Covered Interest Rate Parity
\[ F_0 = S_0 \cdot e^{(r_d - r_f)T} \]

Where \(r_d\) = domestic rate, \(r_f\) = foreign rate. The currency with the higher interest rate trades at a forward discount.

EUR/USD Example: In the EUR/USD pair, EUR is the Base (Foreign) currency and USD is the Quote (Domestic) currency.
Formula: \(F_{\text{EUR/USD}} = S_{\text{EUR/USD}} \cdot e^{(r_{\text{USD}} - r_{\text{EUR}})T}\)
• If USD rate (\(r_{\text{USD}}\)) is 5% and EUR rate (\(r_{\text{EUR}}\)) is 3%, then \(r_d - r_f = +2\%\).
• The Forward rate is higher than Spot (e.g. 1.10 Spot becomes ~1.122 Forward in 1Y).
• The USD (higher yield currency) trades at a forward discount (buys less EUR in the future). The EUR trades at a forward premium.
4. Commodity — Full Cost of Carry with Convenience Yield
\[ F_0 = S_0 \cdot e^{(r + \lambda - c)T} \]
SymbolNameSign Impact
rRisk-free rate↑ F₀ (financing cost)
λStorage costs↑ F₀ (cost to hold)
cConvenience yield↓ F₀ (benefit of holding spot)
🔑 The convenience yield captures the non-monetary benefit of holding the physical commodity (e.g., ability to keep a refinery running). High convenience yield → Backwardation.
Arbitrage Enforcement — Cash and Carry

The formula is enforced by two arbitrage strategies:

If F₀ > S₀·e^(rT) — Cash and Carry
  1. Borrow S₀ at r
  2. Buy spot asset
  3. Enter short forward at F₀
  4. At T: deliver asset, receive F₀, repay loan → risk-free profit
If F₀ < S₀·e^(rT) — Reverse Cash and Carry
  1. Short sell spot asset, receive S₀
  2. Invest S₀ at r
  3. Enter long forward at F₀
  4. At T: receive S₀e^(rT), pay F₀ for asset to close short → risk-free profit
Numerical Example
Given

\(S_0 = 100\), \(r = 5\%\), dividend yield \(q = 2\%\), \(T = 0.5\) years (6-month equity index forward)

\[ F_0 = 100 \cdot e^{(0.05 - 0.02) \times 0.5} = 100 \cdot e^{0.015} \approx \mathbf{101.51} \]

The fair forward price is 101.51. If the market price deviates from this, an arbitrage opportunity exists.

🔍 Case A: Market Price is 103 (Overpriced) — Cash-and-Carry Arbitrage

Opportunity: Actual Forward (\(103\)) > Fair Forward (\(101.51\)). The forward is overpriced.

Arbitrage Strategy (Cash-and-Carry):

  1. At \(t=0\): Borrow \(100\) at \(r=5\%\), buy the index at \(S_0=100\), and short the forward contract at \(F_{\text{mkt}} = 103\).
  2. During \(T\): Collect dividends at \(q=2\%\).
  3. At maturity \(T=0.5\): Sell the index via the forward contract for \(+103.00\). Repay the loan: \(-100 \cdot e^{0.05 \times 0.5} \approx -102.53\). Receive accumulated dividends: \(+100 \cdot (e^{0.02 \times 0.5}-1) \approx +1.01\).
  4. Risk-free Net Profit: \(103.00 - 102.53 + 1.01 = \mathbf{1.48}\) today.
🔍 Case B: Market Price is 99 (Underpriced) — Reverse Cash-and-Carry Arbitrage

Opportunity: Actual Forward (\(99\)) < Fair Forward (\(101.51\)). The forward is underpriced (this is what happens if it trades at 99).

Arbitrage Strategy (Reverse Cash-and-Carry):

  1. At \(t=0\): Short-sell the index at \(S_0=100\), invest the \(100\) proceeds at \(r=5\%\), and enter a long forward contract at \(F_{\text{mkt}} = 99\).
  2. During \(T\): You must pay the dividend yield of \(q=2\%\) to the lender of the index.
  3. At maturity \(T=0.5\): Receive the matured investment: \(+100 \cdot e^{0.05 \times 0.5} \approx +102.53\). Buy the index via the long forward for \(-99.00\) to close the short. Pay the accumulated dividends: \(-100 \cdot (e^{0.02 \times 0.5}-1) \approx -1.01\).
  4. Risk-free Net Profit: \(102.53 - 99.00 - 1.01 = \mathbf{2.52}\) today.
Spot-Forward Parity — Intuition

Spot-Forward Parity says two portfolios must have the same value today:

Portfolio A
Long forward + PV(F₀) in cash
Buy bond that pays F₀ at T; enter long forward
Portfolio B
Hold spot asset
Both deliver one unit of asset at T
\[ S_0 = F_0 \cdot e^{-rT} + \text{PV(income)} \implies F_0 = (S_0 - I) \cdot e^{rT} \]
💡 Forward price = expectation under \(\mathbb{Q}\), not \(\mathbb{P}\). Under the risk-neutral measure, the expected return on every asset is the risk-free rate. So \(F_0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T]\). Under the real-world measure, the expected future spot price differs due to the risk premium: \(F_0 \neq \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[S_T]\).
Quick Interview Reminder:
• \(\mathbb{P}\) (Physical / Real-World Measure): Actual real-world probabilities. Investors are risk-averse and demand a risk premium, meaning risky assets have an expected return \(\mu > r\).
• \(\mathbb{Q}\) (Risk-Neutral Measure): A mathematical pricing tool. We assume investors are risk-neutral (no risk premium is demanded), meaning all assets earn exactly the risk-free rate \(r\).
Payoffs at Maturity
Long Forward — Payoff
\[ \Pi_{\text{long}} = S_T - F_0 \]

Unlimited upside. The long wins every dollar above F₀ and loses every dollar below. There is no floor (unlike a call option — no premium paid here).

Short Forward — Payoff
\[ \Pi_{\text{short}} = F_0 - S_T \]

The short profits when the spot falls. Losses are theoretically unlimited (\(S_T\) can rise without bound). Risk is symmetric around F₀.

Value of a Forward Contract During Its Life

At inception, V₀ = 0. But as time passes and the spot price moves, the forward develops mark-to-market value:

\[ V_t^{\text{long}} = S_t - F_0 \cdot e^{-r(T-t)} = (F_t - F_0) \cdot e^{-r(T-t)} \]

Where \(F_t = S_t \cdot e^{r(T-t)}\) is the current forward price for delivery at T. The PV factor discounts the gain/loss back to today.

⚠️ Interview classic: You entered a 1-year forward to buy gold at $1,900. Six months later, gold spot = $2,000 and r = 4%. What is the MTM value?
\(F_t = 2000 \cdot e^{0.04 \times 0.5} = 2040.4\)
\(V_t = (2040.4 - 1900) \cdot e^{-0.04 \times 0.5} \approx \mathbf{\$137.6}\)

Lifecycle of a Futures Position

Day 0 — Inception

Trader posts Initial Margin (IM) to the clearing broker. Agrees on standardised contract size, delivery date, and underlying. Account balance = IM.

Daily — Mark-to-Market Settlement

Exchange calculates daily settlement price. Gains are credited, losses debited from margin account. Daily PnL = ΔPrice × Multiplier × #Contracts.

Margin Call Day

If account balance falls below Maintenance Margin (MM), broker issues a margin call. Trader must deposit Variation Margin = IM − Current Balance to restore to IM.

Rollover

Before expiry, position is "rolled" — close the near contract, reopen in the next contract. Generates roll yield (positive in backwardation, negative in contango).

Expiry — Close or Delivery

~95% of futures are closed before expiry. Remaining result in physical delivery (commodities) or cash settlement (equity index).

Key Interview Points — Margin
⚡ The Restoration Rule: After a margin call, you restore to Initial Margin, NOT to Maintenance Margin (VM = IM − Balance).
Why? The exchange wants to force a buffer (cushion). If you only restored to MM, you would be sitting right on the edge of the trigger threshold. A tiny market fluctuation the next day would trigger another margin call, causing administrative burdens and execution friction.
🚩 Interview trap: If balance = 2,100 and MM = 2,000, there is no margin call even though you lost money. Only trigger when Balance < MM.
💡 Short positions: PnL is inverted. A price increase is a loss for the short. Same margin mechanics apply.
🏦 Liquidation: If the trader cannot post variation margin, the broker liquidates the position at market price to prevent a negative balance.
Leverage Calculation
\[ \text{Leverage} = \frac{\text{Nominal Value}}{\text{Initial Margin}} = \frac{S_0 \times \text{Multiplier} \times N}{\text{IM}} \]

Example: Index at 5,000, multiplier €10/pt, IM = €2,700 → Nominal = €50,000 → Leverage = 18.5×. A 1% adverse move costs €500 — 18.5% of your margin.

⚠️ Cash drag (institutional context): Funds running large futures books must hold a liquidity buffer in cash to meet daily MTM without forced selling of portfolio assets. This "drag" reduces the effective return on the portfolio.
Forward Curve — Contango vs Backwardation
Contango — F > S

The forward curve is upward-sloping. Futures price exceeds spot. The market is pricing in financing and storage costs exceeding any convenience yield.

Examples: Gold (always), crude oil in normal supply environment.

Backwardation — F < S

The forward curve is downward-sloping. Spot exceeds futures. High convenience yield (physical scarcity) or strong expectations of future price declines.

Examples: Oil during supply shock, agricultural commodities at harvest.

Roll Yield

As expiry approaches, the futures price converges to the spot (\(F \to S_T\)). A long position that holds through time earns (or loses) a roll yield from this convergence.

Contango → Negative Roll

Roll a long: sell cheap expiring contract, buy more expensive next contract. You constantly pay up. ETF investors in VIX/crude oil feel this acutely.

Backwardation → Positive Roll

Roll a long: sell expensive expiring contract, buy cheaper next contract. You lock in a structural gain. Commodity trend funds exploit this.

Interview "Aha!" Moments
Why is Gold always in Contango?

Gold has storage costs, financing costs, and almost zero convenience yield — you can't "use" gold to keep a factory running. So \(r + \lambda > c\) always, and F > S.

Why does Oil flip into Backwardation?

Supply shocks. Refineries need physical crude now. The convenience yield spikes, flipping the curve into backwardation. OPEC cuts → backwardation signal.

Keynes' "Normal" Backwardation

Producers (naturally short) offer speculators a discount F₀ < E[\(S_T\)] as an insurance premium. Speculators are compensated for taking on price risk. This creates a structural positive roll yield for long-only commodity investors.

VIX ETF Roll Drag

VIX futures are almost permanently in contango (volatility mean-reverts, so near-term vol < long-term vol). This explains why products like VXX persistently lose value over time: they bleed ~5-10% per month in roll costs.

Basis Risk
\[ \text{Basis} = S - F \]

Basis risk is the risk that the hedge instrument and the hedged asset don't move in perfect lockstep. Even if you hedge perfectly in notional, residual basis risk means P&L is not fully offset.

Contango → Negative Basis
Basis < 0
F > S, market expects rising prices or high carry costs
Backwardation → Positive Basis
Basis > 0
S > F, physical scarcity or declining price expectation

Minimum Variance Hedge Ratio

To minimise the variance of the hedged portfolio, you don't always hedge 1:1. The optimal hedge ratio scales for the relationship between the spot asset and the futures instrument:

\[ h^* = \rho_{SF} \cdot \frac{\sigma_S}{\sigma_F} \]
SymbolMeaning
\(h^*\)Optimal hedge ratio (units of futures per unit of spot exposure)
\(\rho_{SF}\)Correlation between changes in spot price and futures price
\(\sigma_S\)Std deviation of changes in spot price
\(\sigma_F\)Std deviation of changes in futures price
Number of Contracts
\[ N^* = h^* \times \frac{V_S}{V_F} \]

Where \(V_S\) is the dollar value of the spot position and \(V_F\) is the dollar value of one futures contract (= Futures Price × Multiplier).

💡 Note that \(h^*\) is estimated via OLS regression of \(\Delta S\) on \(\Delta F\). The slope coefficient IS the hedge ratio, and \(R^2\) tells you what fraction of spot variance is hedged. The residual variance is the irreducible basis risk.
Worked Example — Jet Fuel Cross-Hedge

An airline needs to hedge 2,000,000 gallons of jet fuel. It will use crude oil futures (contract size: 42,000 gallons = 1,000 barrels). Estimated: \(\rho = 0.92\), \(\sigma_{jet} = 0.032\), \(\sigma_{crude} = 0.028\).

\[ h^* = 0.92 \times \frac{0.032}{0.028} = 1.051 \] \[ N^* = 1.051 \times \frac{2{,}000{,}000}{42{,}000} \approx \mathbf{50 \text{ contracts}} \]
🔑 The airline needs 50 short crude oil futures contracts — slightly more than the 1:1 naive hedge of 47.6, because jet fuel is more volatile than crude. The 8% basis risk (1 − ρ² = 0.153 ≈ 15% of variance unhedged) reflects the crude/jet spread risk.
Hedging an Equity Portfolio with Index Futures
\[ N^* = \frac{\beta_P \cdot V_P}{V_F} \]
SymbolMeaning
\(N^*\)Number of futures contracts required
\(\beta_P\)Current beta of the portfolio (measure of systematic risk)
\(\beta^*\)Target beta of the portfolio (e.g., \(0\) to fully hedge)
\(V_P\)Total market value of the equity portfolio
\(V_F\)Notional value of one index futures contract (\(= \text{Futures Price} \times \text{Multiplier}\))

To eliminate all systematic risk, short \(N^*\) index futures (target \(\beta^* = 0\)). To change the portfolio beta from \(\beta_P\) to a target \(\beta^*\):

\[ N^* = \frac{(\beta^* - \beta_P) \cdot V_P}{V_F} \]

A negative \(N^*\) indicates shorting futures (reducing beta), while a positive \(N^*\) indicates going long futures (increasing beta). This allows portfolio managers to make rapid tactical overlays without incurring the transaction costs of trading the underlying stocks.
