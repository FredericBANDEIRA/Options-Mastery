The Vol Quoting Convention — "Vols not Dollars"

In professional markets, options are never quoted in dollar terms. They are quoted in implied volatility (vols):

Dollar price quote
"This call is $2.50"
Meaningless without knowing S, K, T. Changes every second as spot moves.
Vol quote
"This is a 20-vol offer"
Self-consistent. Automatically "correct" as spot moves. Directly comparable across strikes/maturities.
💡 Interview gotcha: "If Vega is highest ATM, why are bid-offer spreads in vol terms wider for OTM options?"
Because OTM options have very low dollar Vega. Even a huge vol move only changes their price by a few cents. Market makers need wider vol spreads on OTM options to earn enough actual dollar profit to justify the liquidity provision risk.

Vega Risk — The Un-Hedgeable Greek

Unlike Delta and Gamma, Vega cannot be hedged with the underlying stock. There is no "volatility asset" to buy in the spot market. To hedge Vega, you must use other options — which introduces basis risk and complexity.

Delta / Gamma
Hedge with stock
Direct linear relationship to S. Buy/sell underlying.
Theta
Hedge with Gamma
"Hedged" by being long Gamma — the two offset in the PDE.
Vega
Must use options
Hedging a 1M option with a 3M option introduces basis risk — their IVs don't move 1:1.
The Vega Matrix — Reality vs Textbook
Textbook view

σ is a single number. Vega is a single number ν. Done.

Trader's reality

Every strike AND every maturity has its own σ(K,T). Vega is a full matrix across the surface. You might be "Vega neutral" in aggregate but short 1M vol and long 1Y vol — a huge difference if the vol term structure shifts.

Vega Bucketing & Stress Testing
Tenor Buckets

Rather than aggregating all Vega, desks break it into buckets by expiry: 1M, 3M, 6M, 1Y, 2Y+. A book might be Vega neutral overall but long 3M vol and short 12M vol. If the term structure flattens, this matters enormously.

Moneyness Buckets (ATM vs Wings)

ATM Vega and OTM "tail" Vega behave differently. A skew steepening (OTM put vol spikes but ATM vol stays flat) will hit your tail Vega bucket but not your ATM bucket. You need to track them separately.

Non-Uniform Stress Testing

Standard models stress by moving all vols up 1% ("parallel shift"). Reality: in a crash, short-term OTM Put vol spikes much harder than long-term ATM vol — this is a "skew steepening" or a "twist."

💡Mention three types: Parallel Shift (all vols +1%), Twist (short-end vol up, long-end down), and Butterfly (wings expand vs belly). This signals you understand the surface as a 3D object, not a scalar.
Model Risk

IV is an output of a model — you back it out of market prices. Your Vega is only as good as your model's calibration. A poorly calibrated surface can show Vega neutrality on paper while hiding real exposure in the OTM wings or in non-standard maturities.

🏦 Hard interview question: "Why would a trader be long Vega on the long end and short Vega on the short end?"
This is a Calendar Spread. The trader is betting that long-term uncertainty is underpriced relative to short-term noise, or earning Theta on the short-dated options while hedging against a macro regime shift. The short-dated position provides carry; the long-dated position provides structural protection.
Vega under Black-Scholes — The N'(d₁) Connection
\[\nu = S\sqrt{T}\,N'(d_1) \qquad \text{and} \qquad \Gamma = \frac{N'(d_1)}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}}\]

Both Vega and Gamma are driven by N'(d₁) — the normal PDF at d₁. This is why they share the same bell shape: peaked ATM where d₁ ≈ 0 (N'(0) = max), decaying to zero in the tails.

💡 Call Vega = Put Vega by put-call parity: since C − P = Se^{-qT} − Ke^{-rT} (a linear forward), the convexity (second derivative with respect to σ) of both must be identical. Intuition: the "uncertainty" built into the bell-curve component of BS is the same whether you hold the right to buy or sell.
The Golden Equation: Vega = σS²T × Gamma
\[\nu = \sigma\,S^2\,T \times \Gamma\]

This is the most powerful relationship in options Greeks — a favourite for technical interviews. It allows instant conversion between Gamma and Vega:

Gamma — short-dated risk
Proportional to 1/√T
Explodes near expiry. Sensitivity to immediate price moves. "Today's P&L."
Vega — long-dated risk
Proportional to √T
Grows with time. Sensitivity to future expected vol. "Long-run regime risk."
⚡ Trading strategy implication:
Want to bet on spot moves (realised vol)? → Buy short-dated ATM options (High Gamma, Low Vega)
Want to bet on market fear (implied vol regime)? → Buy long-dated options (Low Gamma, High Vega)
Vega as "Future Gamma" — The Conceptual Intuition

One of the most interview-worthy insights in derivatives:

\[\nu \approx \text{weighted sum of expected future Gamma profits over remaining life of option}\]

When you buy an option, you're buying the right to profit from Gamma (realised moves). If IV increases, the market is saying: "We expect more future price movement." More expected movement → more expected Gamma profits → option worth more today. That increase in value IS Vega.

🧠 Interview answer: "Vega is the integral of expected Gamma profits over the remaining life of the option. An increase in IV boosts expected future variance, and Gamma is the tool we use to capture that variance. Buying long-dated Vega is buying the expectation of future Gamma scalping opportunities."

Key Observations
√T Rule — Vega grows with time

A 3Y option has ~√(3/0.25) ≈ 3.5× more Vega than a 3M option ATM. Short-dated options have almost zero Vega — a 1-day option doesn't care about vol because there's no time left for it to manifest.

\[\nu = S\sqrt{T}\,N'(d_1) \propto \sqrt{T}\]
Lognormal Asymmetry — Right tail is fatter

The Vega profile is not perfectly symmetric around K. The right tail (high spot) lingers longer because in a lognormal world, stock prices can go to ∞ but are floored at 0. An option at S=150 has more residual optionality than at S=50 (with K=100), because the potential dollar upside is theoretically uncapped.

Vega Sensitivities — Volga & The Gamma-Vega Tug of War
Volga (Vomma) — Vega's Convexity
\[\text{Volga} = \frac{\partial\nu}{\partial\sigma} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial\sigma^2}\]

For OTM options, Vega is convex in volatility — as vol rises, the Vega of OTM options increases (they become "more alive"). This is Volga (also called Vomma), the vol-of-vol Greek.

💡Brainteaser answer: "You're long a deep OTM call. Vol spikes. What happens to your Vega?" → Your Vega increases. Positive Volga. The distribution widens, making your OTM strike suddenly much more relevant. You become "more long" volatility precisely when vol is rising — convexity in vol space.

For ATM options, Vega is nearly linear in volatility — their Vega barely changes when vol moves 20% to 30%. But for OTM options, the nonlinearity is significant. This is why OTM options are "Volga plays."

The Gamma-Vega Tug of War — A Classic Interview Trap

Question: "I want to hedge the Vega of a 1Y option using 1M options. What's the main risk?"

Answer: To get enough Vega from 1M options to match the 1Y position, you need a massive quantity of the short-dated contracts (because 1M Vega = √(1/12) × ATM_Vega ≈ 29% of 1Y Vega).

That massive position brings enormous Gamma and Theta exposure. Short-dated options have very high Gamma-to-Vega ratios — so you're taking on a huge amount of Gamma risk you didn't want, just to hedge Vega.

\[\frac{\Gamma}{\nu} = \frac{1}{\sigma S^2 T} \propto \frac{1}{T} \implies \text{short-dated options have high } \Gamma/\nu \text{ ratio}\]
⚠️This is why vol desks use Variance Swaps to hedge Vega cleanly — they are pure vol instruments with no spot or Gamma exposure, eliminating the Gamma-Vega tug of war entirely.
