Theta — Time Decay

The "rent" you pay to own Gamma. The certain daily loss on a long option position from the passage of time alone.

The Gamma-Theta Symbiosis — No Free Lunch

In the Black-Scholes world, Gamma and Theta are the two sides of the same coin. You cannot have one without paying for the other:

Long Gamma (Γ > 0)

You profit from realised volatility. Buy the dips, sell the rallies. Every move generates ½Γ(dS)² of P&L. The "paycheck."

Negative Theta (Θ < 0)

The "rent" you pay for that Gamma. Every day that passes, the option loses time value — even if spot doesn't move. The certain, daily cost.

🎯 Classic interview answer — "How do you make money if you're long Gamma but the market is flat?"
You don't. You bleed Theta every day. Being long Gamma is a bet that realised vol will be high enough to offset the daily time decay. A flat market means you lose the rent without collecting the paycheck.
The Black-Scholes Theta Decomposition

Theta splits into two economically distinct components:

\[ \Theta_{\text{call}} = \underbrace{-\frac{S\,\sigma\,N'(d_1)}{2\sqrt{T}}}_{\text{Pure Time Decay}} \underbrace{- r\,K\,e^{-rT}\,N(d_2)}_{\text{Carry Effect (call)}} \]
Term 1 — Pure Decay (Diffusion)
−Sσ·N'(d₁) / 2√T
Always negative. Driven by N'(d₁) — peaks ATM. This is the erosion of pure optionality. The Theta-Gamma relationship: Θ ≈ −½σ²S²Γ
Term 2 — Carry Effect
±rKe^(−rT)·N(±d₂)
Call: negative (PV of strike payment increases). Put: can be positive for deep ITM — you're waiting to receive K, and time makes that receipt closer in PV terms.
The Bleed Equation — Linking Θ and Γ
\[ \Theta \approx -\frac{1}{2}\,\sigma^2\,S^2\,\Gamma \]

For a delta-neutral portfolio with zero interest rates. This is the core relationship: Theta and Gamma are proportional. High Gamma = high Theta. You always pay for convexity.

High σ effect
↑|Θ| and ↓Γ
σ² dominates in the equation — higher vol → more Theta despite lower Gamma
High Γ effect
↑|Θ| directly
More convexity = more daily rent. They scale together.
Break-even condition
RV = IV
Daily Gamma P&L = Daily Theta cost only when realised vol equals implied vol

Non-Linearity — The Theta Cliff

Time decay is not linear. The rate of decay accelerates as expiry approaches — driven by the 1/√T term in the formula:

\[\Theta \propto -\frac{1}{\sqrt{T}} \implies \text{as } T \to 0,\; |\Theta| \to \infty\]
Early in life (T large)
Small daily decay
One day lost is a tiny fraction of total remaining optionality. The stock still has time to move ITM.
Near expiry (T small)
Accelerating decay
Every hour represents a massive chunk of remaining uncertainty. ATM options enter "Theta cliff" mode.

Path Dependency — "We Only Know Theta Today"

A critical interview insight: while we know the option will have zero time value at T=0, the path of decay depends entirely on where spot goes:

If option stays ATM
Theta cliff
Decay accelerates to the very last second. Most painful scenario for a long option holder.
If option moves ITM
Decay slows
Behaves more like a forward. Less "optionality" left to decay. Theta burden eases.
If option moves OTM
Decay slows
Converging to zero — but the option is also nearly worthless. Theta becomes irrelevant.
⚠️ The trap: You cannot simply divide the total premium by the number of days to get your daily Theta cost. The actual daily cost depends on moneyness at each future point — which is path-dependent and unknowable today.

If the stock realises vol in those final hours that exceeds the implied vol you paid, the Gamma P&L overwhelms the Theta cost. Near-expiry long-Gamma bets are pure vol plays on imminent events.
Theta vs Moneyness — The Inverse Bell Curve

When r = q = 0, Theta is a symmetric inverse bell curve — peaked (most negative) at ATM, approaching zero at deep ITM and OTM. When rates or dividends are non-zero, the carry effect distorts the tails.

The Carry Effect — When Theta Goes Positive
High r — Deep ITM Put gets Positive Theta

A deep ITM put gives you the right to receive K. As time passes, the present value of that future receipt increases — this is a benefit that can outweigh the pure optionality decay.

💡Result: Deep ITM European Puts can have Θ > 0. You make money just by sitting still — time is on your side because you're effectively holding a bond that matures sooner each day.
🚩Early exercise trigger: Positive Theta ↔ Negative Time Value ↔ Option below intrinsic. For American puts: exercise immediately and earn r on the cash received.
High q — Deep ITM Call gets Positive Theta

Dividends cause the forward price Se^{-qT} to be below spot. As T→0, the forward converges upward to spot. For deep ITM calls, this "convergence effect" — capturing the dividend discount — can create positive Theta.

💡You are capturing the "recovery" of the price drop that was priced into the forward. Conversely, high q makes deep ITM Put Theta more negative (the dividend hurts the put's forward component).

🎯 Interview question: "I'm long a deep ITM Put and Theta is positive. What does that tell me about Time Value?"
Answer: Time Value is negative (Price < Intrinsic Value). For an American option, I should exercise immediately to capture the intrinsic value and invest the proceeds at the risk-free rate.
Theta & Volatility — "Rent" vs Opportunity
\[\Theta \approx -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma \implies \text{Higher } \sigma \implies \text{Higher } |\Theta|\]

Higher vol means a more expensive option. Since that option must reach zero time value by maturity, the daily decay (|Θ|) must be larger. The paradox: higher vol also means lower ATM Gamma — but σ² dominates in the equation above.

Low Vol — Sharp Needle

Theta is nearly zero unless you're exactly at the strike. Decay is highly concentrated. The "uncertainty" is narrow — only the tiny zone around K bleeds meaningfully.

High Vol — Wide, Deep Bowl

Theta is larger (deeper) and wider. You don't have to be precisely ATM to experience heavy decay. Even significantly OTM/ITM options bleed because the high vol keeps uncertainty — and thus time value — alive across a wide spot range.

The BS PDE is not just a pricing formula — it's a break-even condition:

\[\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0\]
ComponentGreekP&L interpretation
\(\partial V/\partial t\)Theta (Θ)The "Rent" — daily time cost

🧠 Interview pro tip: When asked "What is the Black-Scholes equation?", don't recite the call pricing formula. Give them the PDE. Explain it as a break-even condition: the time decay of an option plus its convexity gain from the "typical" σ√dt move must equal the risk-free return of the delta-hedged portfolio. This is the answer that separates interns from analysts.
Sampling Realised Vol — The Core of Delta-Hedging P&L
\[\text{Daily P\&L} \approx \frac{1}{2}S^2\Gamma\left(\sigma_R^2 - \sigma_{IV}^2\right)dt\]
σ_R > σ_IV
Profit

σ_R = σ_IV
Break-even

You overpaid for vol. Theta bled you more than Gamma earned.
⚡ Stress test Q: "Long Gamma, market gaps +10% overnight. Good or bad?"
Excellent. A gap = massive realised vol in one move. Your Gamma P&L (½Γ·gap²) will dwarf the single night of Theta. Gaps are the long-Gamma trader's best friend.

Stress test Q: "Long option, flat spot, IV drops 5%. What happens?"
You lose on both fronts simultaneously: Theta bleeds your time value AND the Vega drop reduces your mark-to-market. Double punishment.
