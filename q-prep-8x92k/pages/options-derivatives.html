📌 Call & Put Fundamentals

Q-OD-01
Answered
What is a European call option? What is a European put option? What is the difference with an American option?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Elevator Pitch

A Call option gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy an underlying asset at a pre-specified strike price (K) on or before a specific maturity date (T). In exchange for this right, the buyer pays an upfront cost called the premium (C).

A Put option gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to sell the underlying asset at the strike price (K) on or before maturity (T) for an upfront premium (P).

📐 Payoff at Maturity (T)

Call Payoff = max(0, ST − K)
Put Payoff = max(0, K − ST)

Where ST is the spot price of the underlying at maturity T, and K is the strike price.

🌍 European vs. American Distinction

  • European Option: Can only be exercised precisely at maturity (T).
  • American Option: Can be exercised at any time between today and maturity (t ∈ [0, T]).
💡 Interview Trap & Follow-Up:
Interviewer: "Which option is more expensive, American or European? And when would you actually exercise an American option early?"
Answer: An American option is always worth at least as much as a European option (CAm ≥ CEur) because it gives extra optionality. However, on a non-dividend paying stock, you should never early exercise an American Call (you forfeit the time value and pay cash early, losing interest on K). For an American Put, early exercise can be optimal if deeply in-the-money and interest rates are high (to collect K immediately and earn interest on cash). If the stock pays dividends, an American Call may be exercised right before the ex-dividend date to capture the dividend!

💡 Interview Pro-Tip & Whiteboard Etiquette:
1. Always draw two lines or clearly state the difference between Gross Payoff vs. Net P&L: Gross Payoff starts exactly at 0 on the y-axis and kinks at K. Net P&L shifts the entire graph down by C (or P) for long positions, and up by C (or P) for short positions.
2. Zero-Bound Trap for Puts: Interviewers love to ask: "Is the max profit on a long call and long put both unlimited?"
Answer: No! Long Call has +∞ profit potential because a stock can theoretically rise forever (ST → +∞). But for a Long Put, the stock price cannot fall below $0 (ST ≥ 0), so the maximum gross payoff is bounded at K, making the net maximum profit exactly K − P.
Q-OD-03
Answered
What is the breakeven of an option?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Elevator Pitch & Standard Formula

The breakeven price (SBE) of an option is the underlying spot price at which the net P&L of the position is exactly zero (Net P&L = 0).

  • Call Breakeven (Long or Short): SBE = K + C
  • Put Breakeven (Long or Short): SBE = K − P
🎯 Quant / Trading Desk Distinction (How to score 10/10 in an interview):
While K + C is the textbook formula at expiration, a top-tier candidate must point out two critical nuances:
1. Time Value of Money (Continuous Capitalization): Because the premium C is paid upfront at time t = 0 and the option expires at maturity T, the exact economic breakeven taking into account the opportunity cost of cash at risk-free rate r is:
SBE (Call) = K + C · erT   |   SBE (Put) = K − P · erT
2. Mid-Life Breakeven (t < T): Prior to expiration, options hold positive Time Value. Therefore, if you hold a long call, you can sell it back to the market at a net profit/breakeven at an underlying spot price significantly lower than K + C!
Q-OD-04
Answered
If you make money when the underlying goes down both when you are long put and short call, what is the difference between the two positions?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Core Distinction: Payoff Profile & Downside Capture

While both positions are bearish (positive P&L when the underlying drops), their payoff structures, risk profiles, and Greeks differ fundamentally:

  • Long Put (Directional Downside Capture): When the stock drops further below K, your profit grows linearly up to a maximum of K − P (as ST → 0). You capture 100% of the ongoing downward move.
  • Short Call (Income / Yield Strategy): When the stock drops below K, the option expires worthless and your profit is strictly capped / constant at the premium received (+C). No matter how deep the market crashes, you can never earn more than $+C$.

⚖️ Comprehensive Comparison Table for Interviewers

Feature / Metric Long Put (Buy Put) Short Call (Sell Call)
Max Profit (if S drops to 0) Substantial (K − P) Capped at Premium (+C)
Max Loss (if S rallies to +∞) Capped at Premium Paid (−P) Infinite (−∞) ⚠️
Delta (Directional Bias) Negative (−Δ approaching −1) Negative (−Δ approaching 0 OTM)
Gamma & Volatility (Vega) Long Gamma (+Γ), Long Vega (+V) Short Gamma (−Γ), Short Vega (−V)
Upfront Cashflow Cash Outflow (Debit / pay P) Cash Inflow (Credit / receive C)
💡 Interview Pro-Tip (When to trade which):
• Buy a Long Put when you anticipate a violent market crash or high-volatility sell-off (you want long Gamma and long Vega to amplify gains).
• Sell a Short Call when you have a neutral-to-moderately bearish view in a stagnant market where implied volatility is overpriced (you want positive Theta / time decay and short Vega to harvest premium).
Q-OD-05
Answered
When and why would you exercise an American option before its expiry? Is the reasoning different for calls vs puts?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Golden Rule: Why Early Exercise is Normally Optimal to Avoid

An option's market price is composed of Intrinsic Value + Time Value (TV > 0). If you early exercise, you only capture the Intrinsic Value and destroy 100% of the remaining Time Value. Therefore, if you wish to exit or monetize an option position prior to maturity, it is almost always mathematically superior to sell the option in the market rather than exercising it.

However, early exercise becomes optimal under specific boundary conditions, and the reasoning differs completely between Calls and Puts:

1. American CALL Options (Trigger: Dividends)

  • Non-Dividend Paying Stocks: You should NEVER early exercise an American call on a non-dividend stock (Merton's Theorem). Why pay cash (K) early when you can keep your cash earning risk-free interest in the bank until maturity?
  • Dividend-Paying Stocks (D): When a stock goes ex-dividend, its share price drops by D. Call option holders do not receive cash dividends, so the call loses value when the stock drops across the ex-dividend date!
  • Mathematical Trigger: You early exercise a call just before the ex-dividend date (last cum-dividend day) if the upcoming dividend D exceeds the remaining time value plus the interest lost on paying strike K early:
    Exercise Call if: D > Time Value + K · (1 − e−r·Δt)

2. American PUT Options (Trigger: Deep ITM + Interest Rates)

  • Does it require dividends? NO! It can be optimal to early exercise an American put even on non-dividend paying stocks!
  • The Time Value of Money Logic: Suppose you own a Put with Strike K = $100, and the company goes bankrupt (S ≈ $1). Your intrinsic value is $99. Since stock prices cannot drop below $0, your intrinsic value cannot grow any larger than $100! If you wait until maturity T to receive that cash, your funds sit dead earning zero interest. If you exercise early today, you receive $99 cash immediately and deposit it to earn risk-free interest r until maturity!
  • Mathematical Trigger: Exercise when the put is Deep In-The-Money (ITM) and the interest earned on cash received exceeds the remaining time value of the put.
🎯 Quick Summary for Whiteboard:
• American Call: Early exercise is driven purely by Dividends (avoiding the ex-dividend stock drop).
• American Put: Early exercise is driven purely by Time Value of Money / Interest Rates (monetizing deep ITM cash immediately to earn interest).
Q-OD-06
Answered
What is a call with strike K = 0? What is the difference between holding such a call and holding the underlying stock directly?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Elevator Pitch & Real-World Trading Names

A Call option with Strike K = 0 has a payoff at maturity equal to max(ST − 0, 0) = ST. Because stock prices cannot be negative, the gross payoff is exactly equal to the spot price of the stock at maturity.

Real-World Trading Names (How to impress your interviewer):
• Prepaid Forward Contract / LEPO (Low Exercise Price Option): Traded extensively on European and Australian exchanges where investors pay the entire forward price upfront.
• Delta-One Access Products / Participatory Notes (P-Notes): Used heavily by global investment banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley) to give hedge funds 100% economic exposure (Δ = 1) to restricted foreign equity markets (e.g., India, China A-shares, Taiwan) where local capital controls or stamp duties prevent direct share ownership.

⚖️ 4 Core Differences vs. Holding Underlying Stock Directly

Dimension Holding Underlying Stock Directly Holding Zero-Strike Call (K = 0)
1. Dividends (q) Receives all cash dividends quarterly right into bank account. Does NOT receive cash dividends paid during the option life. Thus, its upfront price is discounted by expected dividends: C0 = S0 · e−qT.
2. Voting Rights Full shareholder voting rights & corporate governance voice. No voting rights whatsoever.
3. Upfront Price (if No Divs) Exactly equal to spot price S0. Exactly equal to spot price S0 (by Put-Call Parity: C − P = S − K·e−rT with K=0, P=0).
4. Counterparty Credit Risk Direct physical/custodial asset ownership. Faces counterparty credit risk of the issuing bank or clearinghouse! ⚠️
💡 Interview Pro-Tip: If asked, "If a stock pays no dividends, why would anyone buy K=0 call instead of stock?"
Answer: To save on local transaction taxes/stamp duties, to bypass foreign ownership quotas (using Delta-One P-Notes), or to obtain leverage/margin efficiencies within prime brokerage accounts!
Q-OD-07
Answered
Breakdown the value of an option into its components. What is intrinsic value vs time value?
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The Fundamental Value Equation

The total market price (premium) of any option is always decomposed into two distinct components:

Total Option Value = Intrinsic Value (IV) + Time Value (Extrinsic Value, TV)

1. Intrinsic Value (IV) — Immediate Exercise Payoff

The value you would capture if you exercised the option right now at the current spot price S. Intrinsic value is strictly non-negative (IV ≥ 0; it can never be less than zero because you are never forced to exercise).

  • Call Intrinsic Value: IV = max(S − K, 0)
  • Put Intrinsic Value: IV = max(K − S, 0)

2. Time Value (TV / Extrinsic Value) — Optionality & Uncertainty

The excess premium above the intrinsic value that the market is willing to pay for the probability / optionality that the underlying spot price will move further in a favorable direction prior to expiration T.

  • Key Drivers: Time Value depends directly on Implied Volatility (σ) (more dispersion = higher chance of large payoff) and Time Remaining to Expiration (τ) (more time = more opportunity to move).
  • Peak Time Value: Time value is strictly maximized when the option is exactly At-The-Money (S = K) because uncertainty about ending ITM vs OTM is at its highest (Delta ≈ 0.50, Gamma & Vega peak).
🎯 Advanced Quant Trap (Can Time Value be NEGATIVE?):
While time value is strictly positive (TV > 0) for American options (since early exercise guarantees at least intrinsic value) and European Calls without dividends, Time Value can actually be NEGATIVE for Deep In-The-Money European Put Options!
Why? Because if a European Put is deep ITM (e.g., S ≈ $1, K = $100), you cannot exercise until maturity T. Due to discounting / time value of money, the present value of the guaranteed $100 strike you receive in the future (K · e−rT) is less than $100 today! Thus, the market price falls below intrinsic value (P < K − S), giving a negative time value!

⚙️ Option Pricing Parameters

Q-OD-08
Answered
What are all the parameters that influence the price of an option? For each one, explain how an increase affects the price of a call and a put.
💡 View Answer & Interview Tips

📌 The 6 Core Pricing Parameters (Black-Scholes / Binomial Inputs)

Every option pricing model relies on exactly 6 underlying parameters. When one parameter increases (holding all others constant), here is how Call and Put prices respond:

Parameter (When Increased ↑) Call Price Effect Put Price Effect Economic Intuition & Mechanism
1. Underlying Spot Price (S ↑) Increase ↑ (+Δ) Decrease ↓ (−Δ) Calls gain intrinsic/time value as probability of ending ITM rises. Puts lose value as they move OTM.
2. Strike Price (K ↑) Decrease ↓ Increase ↑ Higher strike means paying more to buy stock (bad for Calls) or receiving more when selling stock (good for Puts).
3. Implied Volatility (σ ↑) Increase ↑ (+V) Increase ↑ (+V) Asymmetric Payoff Convexity: Higher dispersion widens both tails. You capture large upside gains while losses remain bounded at $0 on the downside. Thus, both Calls and Puts gain value!
4. Time to Expiration (T ↑) Increase ↑ (+Θ)* Increase ↑ (+Θ)* More time allows greater probability for the stock to make a large favorable move. (*Nuance: Deep ITM European Puts or high-dividend options can lose value with more time due to discounting/carry).
5. Risk-Free Interest Rate (r ↑) Increase ↑ (+ρ) Decrease ↓ (−ρ) Time Value of Money: Call buyers defer paying K until maturity (cash earns interest in bank; PV of strike K·e−rT drops). Put buyers wait to receive cash K (heavy discounting reduces its PV today).
6. Dividend Yield (q ↑) Decrease ↓ Increase ↑ Dividends cause the underlying stock price to drop across ex-dividend dates (S·e−qT). Lower future stock prices hurt Calls and boost Puts.
💡 Whiteboard Etiquette: Never just memorize the arrows (+ − + + + −)! When an interviewer asks you about parameters, always start by stating: "The 6 parameters are S, K, σ, T, r, q..." and immediately follow up with the economic intuition for Why Volatility and Rates work the way they do, as those are the two most frequently probed follow-ups.
